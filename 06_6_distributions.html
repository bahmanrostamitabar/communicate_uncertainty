15  Distributions

Probability distributions are the most informationally complete way to represent uncertainty about a quantity. Rather than summarising the range of possible outcomes through a single number, an interval, or a set of shaded bands, a distributional display represents the full probability assigned to every possible value of the uncertain quantity — showing not just the range but the shape, the central tendency, the skewness, and the weight of the tails. As European Food Safety Authority et al. (2019) define them, distributions are graphical representations of “probabilities for different values of an uncertain quantity that has a single true value,” most commonly presented as a Probability Density Function (PDF), a Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF), or a Complementary Cumulative Distribution Function (CCDF). Additional visual formats include density strips — narrow rectangles in which colour intensity encodes probability density — and violin plots, which combine a mirrored density display with summary statistics. As Bles et al. (2019) note, “density strips are the most accurate representation of the underlying probability distribution around the point estimate,” making them valuable when fidelity to the full distributional shape is a priority.

The distinguishing feature of all these formats is that they communicate the shape of uncertainty, not just its extent. Two forecasts might share the same 90% prediction interval yet have very different distributional shapes — one symmetrical, one heavily right-skewed; one unimodal, one bimodal. Only a distributional display can convey this distinction, and only a distributional display allows the audience to read off any quantile of interest without additional calculation. This completeness makes distributional formats the most powerful tool in the uncertainty communicator’s repertoire — and the most demanding to use responsibly.

15.0.1 Practical Example

Distributional displays have been applied in several high-stakes scientific and policy contexts. In climate science, the UK Climate Projections use paired distributions — a probability density function above a cumulative distribution function, sharing the same horizontal axis — to represent forecast uncertainty for change in maximum temperature over a specific region and time horizon (Spiegelhalter et al., 2011). This pairing of PDF and CDF allows the viewer to read both the relative likelihood of different outcomes (from the PDF) and the cumulative probability up to any threshold (from the CDF) from a single composite figure.

In food safety and agriculture, the European Food Safety Authority uses PDF graphs to communicate uncertainty derived from expert knowledge elicitation, such as the distribution “quantifying uncertainty about how many dairy cows out of a hundred on average are pregnant when slaughtered in the EU” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). These applications are notable because they involve genuine epistemic uncertainty — the product of imperfect data and expert judgement — rather than population variability, and the distributional format is used precisely to represent the full range of what is not known.

In experimental risk communication research, Edwards et al. (2012) tested the ability of lay and expert users to extract probability information from five graphical formats — PDF, CDF, CCDF, a modified CDF, and a three-dimensional PDF — using hypothetical scenarios including E. coli outbreaks, radiation treatments, airport screening, and flood depth predictions. The results of these experiments provide some of the most direct evidence available on which distributional formats best serve which communication tasks, and are drawn on extensively in the good practice recommendations below.

15.0.2 Strengths and Limitations

Strengths. The primary strength of distributional displays is their informational completeness. Unlike intervals, which communicate only the extent of uncertainty, distributions provide exhaustive detail about its shape — enabling audiences to “read off approximate estimates for quantiles other than those reported explicitly” and to assess whether the distribution is symmetric, skewed, bimodal, or heavy-tailed (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). This is particularly important when the shape of the distribution carries decision- relevant information that an interval would suppress: for example, when the risk of an adverse outcome is concentrated in a heavy lower tail rather than distributed symmetrically around the central estimate.

A second strength is the capacity of distributional displays to correct motivated or uninformed misinterpretation of simpler formats. When numerical ranges are presented without distributional context, audiences frequently infer a uniform distribution — assuming that all values within the interval are equally probable. A simple density graphic accompanying an uncertainty range corrects this assumption and decreases the inter-individual variance in distributional perceptions, producing more consistent and better-calibrated interpretations across different audience members (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).

A third advantage concerns the accuracy of specific probability extraction tasks. Edwards et al. (2012) found that Complementary Cumulative Distribution Functions — which plot the probability that the outcome exceeds a given value — “led to the highest probability estimation accuracy” among the formats tested, when the task required users to estimate the probability that a quantity exceeded a specified threshold.

Limitations. Against these strengths must be set several well-documented and serious limitations. The most fundamental is cognitive complexity. “Graphical representations of distributions are difficult to interpret for non-technical audiences” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019), and even technically trained users are not immune to characteristic errors. The most consistently documented is the confusion between the mean and the mode: when asked to identify the mean of a displayed PDF, audiences systematically select the mode — the peak of the distribution — “unless the mean was explicitly marked” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). Because the mean and mode coincide only for symmetric distributions, this error can produce substantially biased interpretations of skewed uncertainty distributions.

A second and more serious error concerns the use of CDFs alone. Ibrekk & Morgan (1987) found that when users were presented with a CDF without additional guidance, some were “severely misled in estimating the mean,” frequently choosing the maximum value of the distribution rather than the central estimate. The CDF’s characteristic rising shape, with its upper asymptote, appears to draw the eye to the maximum rather than the midpoint.

A third limitation is the confusion of uncertainty with variability. Because distributions are frequently used in statistics to map population variance, audiences routinely mistake epistemic uncertainty distributions — which represent a forecaster’s knowledge about a single unknown value — for descriptions of population heterogeneity (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). This misinterpretation is both common and consequential, and is difficult to eliminate through graphical means alone.

Finally, more exotic distributional formats actively harm decision-making. Edwards et al. (2012) found that three-dimensional PDFs — which add a superfluous third visual dimension to a two-dimensional quantity — “led to poor accuracy” across all measured outcomes, the worst performance of any format tested.

Disagreement in the literature. A direct empirical disagreement exists between Ibrekk & Morgan (1987) and Edwards et al. (2012) regarding the utility of CDFs for probability estimation. Ibrekk & Morgan (1987) found that CDFs allowed for “very good estimations of the probability that a value exceeds a threshold,” while Edwards et al. (2012) found that “CDFs led to poor accuracy” in similar tasks — a direct contradiction noted explicitly in European Food Safety Authority et al. (2019). This disagreement is unresolved in the literature and may reflect differences in the specific tasks tested, the populations studied, or the visual presentation of the CDFs used in each experiment. It underscores the importance of empirical testing in specific communication contexts rather than relying on general recommendations.

15.0.3 When to Use This Method

Full distributional displays — PDFs, CDFs, and related formats — should be restricted to technical-level audiences who require precise information about distributional shape, tail probabilities, or quantiles not explicitly reported in simpler summaries (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019; Spiegelhalter et al., 2011). Two-dimensional distributions, in particular, “should be used only at the technical level, and then with great care and explanation” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).

The CCDF format should be preferred when the specific communication task involves helping users estimate the probability that a quantity exceeds a particular threshold — for example, the probability that a flood exceeds a specified depth, or that an emission level exceeds a regulatory limit (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).

For non-technical audiences, full distributional displays should be avoided. Instead, communicators should provide the central estimate alongside a simplified prediction interval or a box plot, with explicit textual accompaniment (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). Density strips may provide a useful middle ground for informed audiences who need more than a simple interval but cannot interpret a full PDF without guidance (Bles et al., 2019).

15.0.4 Good Practice

  • Pair PDFs and CDFs: To mitigate the limitations of either format alone, plot a CDF directly above a PDF with the same horizontal axis scale, allowing users to cross-reference between the two representations (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). This pairing is specifically recommended as best practice by European Food Safety Authority et al. (2019) for technical-level communications.
  • Explicitly mark the central estimate: Never require the audience to visually estimate the mean or median from the shape of the distribution. Always clearly mark the location of the central estimate — and optionally the 5th, 25th, 75th, and 95th percentiles — on both curves in a paired display (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Accompany with textual explanation: Distributional displays must not stand alone. They should always be accompanied by sufficient textual explanation, in the simplest terms possible, that explicitly defines what the height and area of the curves represent and what the viewer should take away from the graphic (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Clarify uncertainty versus variability: Because audiences routinely misinterpret epistemic uncertainty distributions as descriptions of population variability, the accompanying text must explicitly state that “the distribution represents uncertainty about the quantity of interest, for which there is a single true value” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Avoid three-dimensional formats: Three-dimensional PDF displays add visual complexity without informational benefit and actively impair decision-making accuracy. They should not be used in any communication context (Edwards et al., 2012).
  • Use density strips for compact density communication: When the full distributional display is too complex for the target audience but simple intervals are too sparse, density strips provide an effective intermediate format that conveys distributional shape in a compact, visually accessible form (Bles et al., 2019).
