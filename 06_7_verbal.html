15  Verbal Expressions of Uncertainty

Verbal expressions of uncertainty use natural language words and phrases — such as “likely,” “possible,” “doubtful,” “almost certain,” or “slight hope” — to convey the probability of an event or the confidence in a scientific assessment without relying exclusively on numerical metrics. These expressions draw on the full richness of linguistic probability — the repertoire of words that human language has evolved to convey degrees of belief, confidence, and expectation — and they are the oldest and most instinctive mode of uncertainty communication available. Natural language expressions may be used alone, with modifiers such as “very” or “extremely,” or with hedges such as “almost” or “probably” that qualify their directional intent (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). In policy and intelligence contexts, standardised sets of verbal expressions are formally codified as “words of estimative probability” — prescribed lexicons that assign specific verbal terms to specific probability ranges with the goal of reducing interpretive variability across analysts and institutions (Mandel & Irwin, 2021).

The appeal of verbal expressions is immediate and intuitive: they are the mode of communication through which all human beings first learn to express uncertainty, and they remain dominant in everyday risk communication from medical consultations to weather forecasts to news reporting. Yet the literature on verbal uncertainty communication is among the most consistently critical in the field, documenting a pattern of systematic misinterpretation, variable interpretation, and audience alienation that places severe limits on the use of verbal expressions as a standalone communication format.

15.0.1 Practical Example

The historical benchmark for verbal probability communication — and for its failure — is Sherman Kent’s 1951 National Intelligence Estimate on Yugoslavia. The estimate used the term “serious possibility” to describe the likelihood of a Soviet attack on Yugoslavia. When Kent subsequently surveyed the analysts who had contributed to the estimate, he discovered that they interpreted the same phrase wildly differently: Kent himself took “serious possibility” to mean approximately a 65% probability, while other analysts gave estimates ranging from 20% to 80% (Mandel & Irwin, 2021). This 60-percentage-point spread in the interpretation of a single phrase prompted Kent to advocate for the standardisation of verbal probability lexicons in intelligence work — an effort that continues today. Modern intelligence agencies including the US Office of the Director of National Intelligence (ODNI), the UK Professional Head of Intelligence Assessment (PHIA), and NATO all maintain standardised verbal scales that map prescribed words onto defined numerical probability ranges (Mandel & Irwin, 2021).

In climate science, the IPCC employs a highly calibrated verbal likelihood scale — including terms such as “virtually certain” (99–100%), “very likely” (90–100%), “likely” (66–100%), “about as likely as not” (33–66%), “unlikely” (0–33%), and “exceptionally unlikely” (0–1%) — to communicate the likelihood of specific future climate outcomes in its assessment reports (Mastrandrea et al., 2010). This scale is among the most carefully designed verbal probability lexicons in science, with each term explicitly defined and consistently applied across thousands of findings across multiple working groups.

In meteorology, weather forecasters routinely use linguistic probability expressions — “slight chance,” “likely,” “a good chance” — to indicate the probability of precipitation or severe weather events in public forecasts (Ripberger et al., 2022). In legal proceedings, the phrase “beyond reasonable doubt” functions as a verbal expression of high certainty, though empirical research shows that jurors interpret it across a very wide numerical range.

15.0.2 Strengths and Limitations

Strengths. Verbal expressions have three genuine strengths that explain their persistence across virtually all domains of uncertainty communication.

The first is naturalness and cognitive ease. People “find it easier and more natural to use words than numbers” when expressing probability, because language is acquired earlier and is the dominant mode of all human communication (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). For senders — the scientists, analysts, and practitioners who generate uncertainty assessments — verbal expressions reduce the cognitive burden of communication and allow nuance to be expressed without the precision that numbers imply.

The second is directionality — a feature of verbal probability expressions that numeric formats cannot replicate. Verbal probability terms possess inherent directional orientation: positively phrased terms (e.g., “small probability of success”) draw attention to the occurrence of an event, while negatively phrased terms (e.g., “unlikely to succeed”) draw attention to its non-occurrence (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). This directionality allows communicators to implicitly guide the audience’s attention toward or away from specific outcomes, providing a framing tool that goes beyond the bare expression of probability.

The third is face-saving flexibility. Verbal expressions are inherently less precise than numbers, and this imprecision can be used strategically: senders can “use terms as a receiver- or self-face-saving strategy, particularly when communicating the chances of adverse outcomes” (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). When a prediction turns out to be wrong, the vagueness of a verbal expression provides insulation against accountability claims in a way that a stated numerical probability does not.

Limitations. Against these strengths must be set four serious and consistently documented limitations.

The most fundamental is imprecision and interpretive variability. The literature “consensually criticises the use of verbal uncertainty expressions alone, which can be responsible for significant misunderstanding” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). The numerical interpretation of common verbal probability terms varies enormously across individuals: the word “possible,” for instance, spans up to 63 percentage points in the interpretations given by surveyed physicians (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). The phrase “beyond reasonable doubt” shows similar variability among jurors. This variability is not random — it is systematic, influenced by the severity of the outcome described, the context of the communication, and the individual’s prior beliefs — and it cannot be eliminated by encouragement to interpret words consistently.

The second limitation is the severity effect: audiences consistently interpret the same verbal probability term as numerically higher when the event it describes is more severe or undesirable (Ripberger et al., 2022). A doctor who says a treatment has a “slight chance” of causing harm may find that patients interpret this as a substantially higher probability than if the same phrase were used to describe a minor inconvenience. This context sensitivity means that verbal expressions cannot be relied upon to convey consistent probability information across different content domains.

The third limitation is the extremity effect: certain linguistic terms drive audiences to assume extreme outcomes. The word “improbable,” for example, is frequently interpreted as meaning impossible — a 0% probability — rather than a low but non-negligible frequency (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019; Ripberger et al., 2022). This error is particularly consequential for risk communication about rare but important events: calling a Black Swan event “improbable” may cause audiences to discount it entirely rather than planning for it at a low level of probability.

The fourth limitation is integration failure: it is cognitively difficult to mathematically combine multiple verbal probability expressions, leading to lower logical coherence and worse aggregation accuracy than numerical formats (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). When a decision requires integrating uncertainty information from multiple sources — each expressed verbally — the resulting judgement is likely to be poorly calibrated relative to what numerical integration would produce.

Disagreement in the literature. The literature reveals a sharp empirical disagreement regarding whether verbal expressions protect or damage communicator credibility. Dhami & Mandel (2022) find that senders who use verbal terms that are directionally consistent with an unexpected outcome — for example, a positive verbal term for a positive outcome that did not occur — “suffered less credibility loss” after a wrong prediction, because the linguistic vagueness shielded them from precise accountability. This suggests that verbal expressions can function as a credibility-protection device.

However, direct experimental evidence from Bles et al. (2020) points in the opposite direction: adding verbal uncertainty caveats to news articles “reduced participants’ trust in the source” compared to using numerical ranges or no uncertainty qualification at all. Verbal hedging, in this study, actively damaged the communicator’s credibility rather than protecting it.

A second disagreement concerns the persistent tension between sender preferences and receiver needs. Forecasters and analysts strongly prefer verbal expressions precisely because of their face-saving properties (National Research Council, 2006), while policymakers and the public consistently prefer numerical precision for high-stakes decisions (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). This sender-receiver mismatch is not merely a difference in stylistic preference: it represents a systematic misalignment between the incentives of communicators and the needs of their audiences.

15.0.3 When to Use This Method

Verbal expressions are best used to summarise the overall quality, strength, or limitations of underlying evidence — what the literature terms meta- uncertainty — where precise quantitative probabilities cannot be legitimately calculated (Bles et al., 2019). They are also effective when the communicator needs to explicitly convey directionality — signalling that a risk is increasing relative to a baseline, or that a finding is stronger than previously reported — rather than a specific probability value (Ripberger et al., 2022).

Verbal expressions should never be used as the sole mode of communicating quantitative probability information when a numerical estimate is available and when the audience needs to make a consequential decision based on the probability (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). In such contexts, they should be used only as a supplement to numerical information, not as a replacement for it.

15.0.4 Good Practice

  • Always pair verbal expressions with numerical translations: Never use a verbal probability expression in isolation. Always include a specific numerical probability or range directly adjacent to the verbal term (Ripberger et al., 2022). The numerical value should appear first, with the verbal label serving as a mnemonic aid rather than the primary communication.
  • Present the number before the word: When combining numerical and verbal formats, present the numerical probability before the verbal term — for example, “20% — unlikely” rather than “unlikely (20%)” — to anchor the audience’s interpretation in the number and reduce the extremity effect associated with the verbal label (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Avoid “improbable” and “unlikely” for rare events: Because non-expert audiences routinely interpret these terms as meaning impossible (0%), communicators should avoid them when describing rare but non-negligible events. Instead, use positively framed terms such as “low probability” or “a small but real chance” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Avoid vague hedging words: Avoid qualifiers such as “about,” “around,” “possible,” or “there is a chance,” as these maximise interpretive variability and degrade comprehension without adding communicative value (Ripberger et al., 2022).
  • Use standardised lexicons where they exist: In domains such as climate science (IPCC likelihood scale) and intelligence analysis (ODNI, PHIA, NATO scales), use the established standardised verbal probability lexicons consistently and cite their numerical definitions explicitly. Deviation from established scales introduces ambiguity that standardisation was designed to eliminate (Mandel & Irwin, 2021).
  • Test interpretation empirically: Before deploying verbal probability expressions in a specific communication context, test the intended audience’s numerical interpretation of the chosen terms. Given the documented variability in interpretation, terms that seem obviously clear to the communicator may be systematically misunderstood by the audience.
