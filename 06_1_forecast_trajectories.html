9  Forecast Trajectories

Forecast trajectories — frequently referred to in the literature as ensemble plots or spaghetti plots — are a visual method for communicating uncertainty by plotting multiple predicted future paths of a variable simultaneously on a single graph (Leffrang & Müller, 2025). Each individual line represents one possible future, generated by varying the input parameters of a forecasting model or by combining outputs from different models. Rather than collapsing the full range of possible futures into a single summary statistic or a smoothed probability band, forecast trajectories present the raw plurality of predictions, allowing the viewer to see both the central tendency and the diversity of possible outcomes simultaneously. The theoretical foundation of this approach is rooted in the frequency format hypothesis, which frames uncertainty as a set of discrete, countable alternative realities rather than as an abstract probability distribution (Leffrang & Müller, 2025). By making individual possible futures visible, the method aims to convey the genuine openness of the future in a way that more abstract representations cannot.

Beyond simply displaying multiple lines, trajectory forecasts are technically distinguished by their ability to capture the correlation structure between successive time steps within a forecasting horizon. Unlike marginal, point-in-time distributions — which describe the range of possible values at each future moment in isolation — trajectory forecasts model the full “path-dependency” of a sequence of outcomes, capturing how earlier values constrain and inform later ones (Kley-Holsteg & Ziel, 2020). Methods such as Gaussian copulas are used to preserve this dependency structure, ensuring that “not only the mean and the marginal properties, but also the correlation structure between hours within the forecasting horizon” are faithfully represented (Kley-Holsteg & Ziel, 2020). This distinction is consequential: for any decision that depends on the cumulative or aggregate behaviour of a variable over time — rather than its value at a single future moment — only a trajectory forecast that preserves the temporal correlation structure can provide reliable probabilistic guidance (Gilbert et al., 2021).

9.0.1 Practical Example

Forecast trajectories have been applied across a wide range of forecasting domains. In meteorology, they are used to visualise the potential tracks of tropical cyclones, with individual paths representing outputs from distinct weather models (Spiegelhalter, 2017). In hydrology, hydrologists use these plots to display river flow over time, tracing the individual predictions of different ensemble members to communicate the uncertainty in flood forecasts (Pappenberger et al., 2013). In public health, ensemble trajectories have been applied to epidemiological forecasting, including the prediction of COVID-19 hospitalisation numbers during the pandemic (Leffrang & Müller, 2025). In demography, population forecasters use trajectories to visualise randomly simulated future fertility rates, allowing users to see historical variability projected forward (Raftery & Ševčíková, 2023).

Three further applications illustrate the practical value of the correlation- preserving properties of trajectory forecasts. In water supply management, Kley-Holsteg & Ziel (2020) use probabilistic multi-step-ahead trajectory forecasts to simulate the aggregate path of demand over a 24-hour horizon, enabling planners to assess “the probability with which a water storage capacity can guarantee the supply over a certain period” — a question that cannot be answered by marginal probability intervals alone. In offshore wind energy operations, Gilbert et al. (2021) use sea-state trajectory forecasts to predict “weather windows” for safely transferring technicians to turbines: because the transfer window requires safe vessel conditions continuously throughout the entire pick-up and return journey, a marginal forecast of conditions at a single time point is insufficient, and only a trajectory forecast can determine whether conditions will remain safe throughout the full operational window. In intraday electricity trading, Serafin et al. (2022) use trajectory forecasts of electricity prices to construct “simultaneous prediction bands” over consecutive trading windows, enabling algorithmic strategies to identify optimal moments to sell renewable energy by detecting when the probability of a price path exceeding a target threshold is sufficiently high.

9.0.2 Strengths and Limitations

Strengths. Forecast trajectories offer several distinctive advantages over summary-based displays. First, individual trajectories capture the true, noisy evolution of a system over time. As Raftery & Ševčíková (2023) note, averaging or smoothing trajectories suppresses the stochastic variation that is a genuine feature of many real-world processes, and a smooth summary trajectory “would be very unlikely” to actually occur — it represents a statistical artefact rather than a plausible future. By contrast, individual trajectories convey the jerky, discontinuous character of actual realisations in a way that interval-based displays cannot.

Second, by explicitly illustrating alternative futures, ensemble trajectories intuitively convey that there is no guaranteed outcome, thereby preventing deterministic anchoring — the tendency of audiences to treat a central estimate as a definitive prediction (Toet et al., 2016). The visual presence of multiple lines signals directly that uncertainty is real and that the future is genuinely open.

Third, visualising discrete ensemble members can raise users’ awareness of severe, unlikely events that might be obscured by summary statistics (Ripberger et al., 2022). A tail trajectory that diverges dramatically from the central cluster makes the possibility of an extreme outcome viscerally present in a way that an interval boundary cannot.

Fourth, for professional users, the visual density of multiple lines implies that a rich web of modelling information has gone into the forecast, which can build confidence in the robustness of the underlying analysis (Pappenberger et al., 2013).

Fifth, and most distinctively for decision contexts that involve cumulative or time-dependent outcomes, trajectory forecasts provide information that no marginal distribution or prediction interval can supply. Kley-Holsteg & Ziel (2020) demonstrate that trajectory forecasts “provide additional information about the expected aggregated or cumulative” outcome over a planning horizon, and Serafin et al. (2022) show that simultaneous prediction bands — which guarantee that a stated percentage of complete price paths lie within specified boundaries over the full horizon — can only be constructed from joint trajectory distributions, not from point-in-time intervals.

Limitations. Against these strengths must be set several well-documented limitations. The most fundamental is visual clutter and cognitive overload: displaying too many trajectories can result in a visual chaos that impairs accurate interpretation, an effect so common that it has given the method its colloquial name (Leffrang & Müller, 2025). This complexity frequently overwhelms general audiences; qualitative studies have documented responses such as “I have no clue which one to believe” when members of the public are shown hurricane ensemble plots (Toet et al., 2016). Gilbert et al. (2021) confirm that raw trajectory visualisations produce “uncertainty information [that] is difficult to interpret by any decision maker” in operational high-stakes scheduling contexts.

A second limitation concerns misdirected attention. While trajectories highlight unlikely alternative futures, they may simultaneously draw attention away from the specific scenarios that forecasters consider most probable (Ripberger et al., 2022). The equal visual weight given to all ensemble members, regardless of their probability, can create a misleading impression of uniform likelihood across the displayed range.

Third, despite the probabilistic intent of ensemble plots, users frequently subvert it by selecting a single trajectory to follow as if it were a deterministic forecast (Pappenberger et al., 2013). This “cherry-picking” defeats the purpose of the ensemble representation.

Fourth, Leffrang & Müller (2025) identify an uncertainty paradox: when uncertainty visualisations are highly effective at depicting extreme volatility, audiences may become alienated and less likely to act on the guidance being communicated.

Fifth, and critically for technical practitioners, traditional point-wise evaluation metrics — including the Mean Absolute Error, Root Mean Squared Error, and the Pinball score — are entirely “blind” to the temporal correlation structure of trajectory forecasts (Kley-Holsteg & Ziel, 2020). They cannot detect whether a simulated path is structurally realistic or has been manipulated to have the correct marginal properties at each time step while exhibiting entirely unrealistic path behaviour. This means that practitioners who evaluate trajectory forecasts using standard metrics may be unknowingly deploying poorly calibrated path forecasts.

Disagreement in the literature. The literature is actively divided on whether ensemble plots ultimately help or harm lay decision-makers. Some researchers argue that they successfully align risk perceptions with actual risk (Leffrang & Müller, 2025), while others find that they confuse users and degrade trust relative to simpler prediction intervals (Leffrang & Müller, 2025). Among practitioners, there is a fundamental disagreement about the value of displaying individual paths: some value the ability to track individual ensemble members, while others argue that such plots are displayed only because they “allow misuse of probabilistic forecasts by following a single line” (Pappenberger et al., 2013).

9.0.3 When to Use This Method

Forecast trajectories are most appropriate when the communication goal requires illustrating the inherent volatility and stochastic variation of a system — particularly when summary intervals would falsely imply a smooth, linear progression inconsistent with the actual character of the process (Raftery & Ševčíková, 2023). They are essential — and irreplaceable by marginal methods — when the decision depends on the cumulative or aggregate behaviour of a variable over multiple time steps, or on whether conditions remain within specified bounds throughout an extended window (Gilbert et al., 2021; Kley-Holsteg & Ziel, 2020).

They are most suitable for technical audiences who are familiar with ensemble methods. For entry-level and informed audiences, they should be used with caution and only after extensive cognitive testing. They are least appropriate when the primary communication goal is a simple behavioural signal or a single actionable threshold — in such cases, the transformation of raw trajectories into categorical probability summaries, as described in the good practice recommendations below, is strongly preferable.

9.0.4 Good Practice

  • Limit the number of trajectories: Restrict the number of plotted lines to prevent visual overload. An excessive number of ensemble members does not enhance decision-making and actively undermines trust (Leffrang & Müller, 2025).
  • Use sequential presentation: Introduce stochasticity gradually — first a small number of individual trajectories, then summary distributions — to help audiences build intuitive understanding before confronting the full ensemble (Raftery & Ševčíková, 2023).
  • Transform raw trajectories into categorical risk summaries: Rather than showing end-users raw trajectory lines, transform the ensemble into operational risk categories (e.g., “Good, Fair, Poor, No-Go”) and visualise the percentage of trajectories in each category at each time step (Gilbert et al., 2021). This distillation into actionable probabilities preserves the information content of the full trajectory forecast while reducing cognitive demand to a level manageable for operational decision-makers.
  • Distinguish probability from visual weight: Make clear that equal visual weight does not imply equal probability. Use visual encoding — line thickness, colour saturation — to convey relative likelihood where trajectories differ in probability.
  • Accompany with a central estimate: Always provide a clearly labelled central estimate or median trajectory alongside the ensemble as a reference point.
  • Use the Energy Score for evaluation: When evaluating whether a trajectory forecast has captured the correct temporal correlation structure, use strictly proper multivariate scoring rules such as the Energy Score or the Variogram Score rather than standard point-wise metrics such as MAE or RMSE, which are entirely insensitive to path dependency (Gilbert et al., 2021; Kley-Holsteg & Ziel, 2020; Serafin et al., 2022).
  • Test with the intended audience: Given the documented confusion effects in non-expert populations, always conduct cognitive testing of ensemble displays with the target audience before deployment.
