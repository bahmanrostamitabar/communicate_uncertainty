13  Categorical Approaches

Categorical approaches communicate uncertainty by classifying data, model outputs, or expert assessments into discrete, ordered qualitative tiers — such as low, medium, and high — or by employing visual symbolic systems that encode these tiers through colour, icon, or label. The most widely recognised example is the Red-Amber-Green (RAG) traffic light system, in which colours convey an ordered progression from acceptable to problematic (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). Other instances include national terrorism threat level scales, IPCC confidence grids, ecological risk matrices, and data pedigree charts that rate the quality of scientific evidence. What unites all these systems is the act of discretisation: continuous probabilistic information, or complex multi- dimensional uncertainty, is compressed into a small number of mutually exclusive categories that are easier to communicate, easier to remember, and easier to act upon — at the cost of the quantitative precision and nuance that the underlying information may possess.

Categorical systems serve a fundamentally different communicative function from the quantitative methods reviewed in the preceding sections. Rather than attempting to convey the magnitude or shape of uncertainty, they aim to convey its significance: whether the level of uncertainty associated with a particular assessment warrants concern, caution, or confidence. As Bles et al. (2019) observe, “indirect uncertainty” — uncertainty about the quality, completeness, or consensus underlying an assessment — is “possibly best amalgamated into a qualitative or ordered categorical scale,” precisely because such uncertainty does not lend itself to numerical expression in the first place.

13.0.1 Practical Example

Categorical approaches are deployed across a strikingly diverse range of domains. In climate risk assessment, the DNV recommended practice for simulation models uses a categorical risk matrix that evaluates the danger of a model failing by combining an uncertainty categorisation — Low, Medium, High — with an assessment of the severity of consequences — Minor to Catastrophic — and mapping the combined result onto a colour-coded “traffic light” grid ranging from green (trusted) to red (not trusted) (Pascoe et al., n.d.). This two-dimensional categorical framework communicates both the degree of uncertainty and its practical importance in a single compact display.

In ecological risk assessment, probabilistic Bayesian network analyses of environmental chemical exposures map the probability of Risk Quotient intervals onto a visual categorical scale where “colours range from green (no risk) to red (posing a risk)” (Moe et al., 2022). This approach bridges the gap between quantitative probabilistic analysis and actionable risk communication, by translating continuous probability outputs into categories that regulatory decision-makers can act on directly.

In intelligence analysis and national security, the US National Intelligence Council communicates analytic confidence using a categorical “high/medium/low scale,” while the UK defines its national Terrorism Threat Level through a categorical scale with verbal labels — where “SEVERE” is defined as a highly likely attack — that is communicated directly to the public through media and government channels (Spiegelhalter, 2017). In science quality assessment, the NUSAP (Numeral, Unit, Spread, Assessment, Pedigree) notational system evaluates the mode of production and quality of scientific information, which “can be represented as a spider or radar chart using traffic light symbols — green for good quality, red for low quality” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).

Finally, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change uses a categorical two-dimensional confidence grid that combines a verbal assessment of evidence strength (“limited,” “medium,” “robust”) with a verbal assessment of scientific agreement (“low,” “medium,” “high”) to communicate the overall confidence in specific findings (Mastrandrea et al., 2010).

13.0.2 Strengths and Limitations

Strengths. Categorical approaches offer two distinctive strengths that quantitative methods cannot replicate. The first is accessibility: evaluative labels such as “high,” “moderate,” and “low” are accessible to lay audiences and can “highlight aspects of uncertainty information that may otherwise be overlooked in more complex numerical displays” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). For audiences with limited statistical literacy, a traffic light system communicates the essential decision-relevant information — is this safe or not? should I be concerned? — without requiring any interpretation of probability values.

The second strength is their suitability for expressing what might be called unquantifiable or indirect uncertainty — the kind that cannot legitimately be assigned a precise numerical probability because it concerns the quality, completeness, or contested nature of the underlying knowledge rather than the probability of a specific outcome. As Spiegelhalter (2017) notes, such “unmodelled uncertainty can only be expressed as a qualitative and subjective assessment.” When the goal is to communicate the pedigree of evidence, the degree of scientific consensus, or the confidence in a model rather than the probability of a specific event, categorical scales are not merely simpler than quantitative formats — they are more epistemically honest.

Limitations. Against these strengths must be set several well-documented and serious limitations. The most significant is what European Food Safety Authority et al. (2019) describe as the “double-edged sword” of evaluative labels: categorical labels can inadvertently signal to a layperson that the categorised element is the only thing that matters, causing them to fixate on the label and “ignore other ways of looking at uncertainty like examining upper and lower values.” By directing attention so powerfully to the category, the system may inadvertently suppress consideration of the quantitative information from which the category was derived.

A second limitation is the conflation of likelihood and confidence. Qualitative categorical scales routinely blur the crucial distinction between the probability of an outcome occurring and the assessor’s confidence in that estimate, because “likelihood implicitly contains confidence levels” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). A risk assessed as “high” might be high because it is likely or because the analysts are very confident it will occur at moderate frequency — two very different epistemic states that the categorical label cannot distinguish.

A third limitation is ambiguity and lack of standardisation. Most categorical scales lack formal operational definitions of the type that exist for probability itself, leading to inconsistent interpretation across different audiences, contexts, and time periods (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). The word “possible” in one categorical framework may correspond to a 20–30% probability; in another, it may indicate any outcome that cannot be excluded. Without explicit quantitative anchoring, categorical labels are susceptible to motivated interpretation.

A fourth limitation is information loss. By forcing continuous probabilistic data into discrete categorical buckets, the system “limits users’ ability to obtain the information that may be most useful” (National Research Council, 2006). All values within a category are treated as equivalent for communication purposes, even if they differ substantially in their underlying probabilities or consequences.

A fifth concern, documented through empirical testing, is that some categorical symbol systems are actively harmful. Attempts to use +/– tables to convey the direction of uncertainty have been found “confusing” by both technical and lay audiences, who “misinterpreted the +/– signs as cancelling each other out” — a response described as finding them “incomprehensible” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).

Disagreement in the literature. The literature highlights a persistent and unresolved conflict between user preferences and comprehension accuracy. Certain professional audiences — including aviation forecasters and industry stakeholders — strongly prefer categorical simplifications because they feel intuitive and actionable (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019; National Research Council, 2006). Yet empirical research “consensually criticises the use of verbal uncertainty expressions alone, which can be responsible for significant misunderstanding” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). The preference for categories appears to reflect their communicative convenience rather than their epistemic accuracy, suggesting that the widespread use of categorical systems may be driven by communication comfort rather than communication effectiveness.

13.0.3 When to Use This Method

Categorical approaches are most appropriate when the goal is to communicate meta-uncertainty — the overall quality, strength, or consensus of the underlying evidence — rather than the precise probability of a specific outcome (Bles et al., 2019; Spiegelhalter, 2017). They are also appropriate when the uncertainty being communicated is genuinely unquantifiable — for example, when expressing the confidence in a model’s structural assumptions, the pedigree of data, or the degree of expert consensus — because in such cases a categorical scale is not an approximation of a more precise quantity but the most honest available representation of the state of knowledge.

Categorical scales should be restricted to “unambiguous events” to prevent audiences from confusing the linguistic ambiguity of the event description with the uncertainty about its probability (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). They should always be supplemented with quantitative information where available, and should never serve as the primary or sole means of communicating numerical probability estimates.

13.0.4 Good Practice

  • Explicitly define all cut-offs: When using evaluative categories such as “high,” “moderate,” and “low,” ensure that the quantitative thresholds for each category are precisely defined and the rationale for the chosen thresholds is transparently explained (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). Vague categorical labels whose boundaries are undefined invite inconsistent interpretation.
  • Provide quantitative translations: Any qualitative category or symbol must be explicitly paired with a quantitative definition of its meaning every time it is used — not only in a technical appendix (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). A table hidden in a footnote is insufficient; the quantitative translation should appear directly alongside the categorical label in the main communication.
  • Supplement with numeric scales where appropriate: Following the InterAcademy Council’s recommendations to the IPCC, qualitative confidence scales “may be supplemented by a quantitative probability scale, if appropriate” (Mastrandrea et al., 2010). This supplementation preserves the accessibility of the categorical display while protecting against the information loss that discretisation inevitably introduces.
  • Avoid +/– symbol systems: Given the documented confusion and misinterpretation associated with directional symbol tables, communicators should not use +/– notation to convey uncertainty direction. If directional information is important, convey it through explicit text or through asymmetric visual displays (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Use two-dimensional categorical matrices with caution: Two-dimensional risk matrices that combine uncertainty with consequence severity — such as the DNV risk matrix — provide more information than one-dimensional categorical scales, but also impose greater cognitive demands. Ensure that both axes are clearly labelled and that the colour scheme is accessible to colour-blind users.
  • Test categorical labels empirically: Because the interpretation of categorical labels is highly context-dependent and can vary substantially across audiences, test the chosen labels with representative members of the target audience before deployment, rather than assuming that terms like “moderate” or “significant” have a shared meaning.
