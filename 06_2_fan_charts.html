10  Fan Charts

Fan charts are visual displays of probabilistic forecasts over time that represent the full range of uncertainty as a series of nested, shaded bands radiating outward from a central estimate — producing the characteristic widening shape from which the method takes its name. The defining visual feature of a fan chart is that “the range of uncertainty inherent in the forecasts broadens with the forecast horizon” (Bank of England, n.d.), an insight that the graphic encodes directly and intuitively: the further into the future the forecast extends, the wider the fan becomes. Typically, the central band represents the modal — most likely — forecast, while surrounding regions of progressively lighter shading or different colours encode decreasing probability bands, representing “the range of possible outcomes and the subjective probabilities that the actual outcomes will fall within each range” (Bank of England, n.d.). As Bles et al. (2019) describe them, fan charts display “the bounds of several different confidence intervals, often coloured to emphasise the changing probability density going further from the point estimate.” Unlike spaghetti plots, which show individual trajectories, fan charts present a smoothed, continuous summary of the full probability distribution across all forecast horizons simultaneously, making the structure of uncertainty immediately visible without requiring the viewer to integrate over multiple individual lines.

10.0.1 Practical Example

The most prominent and widely studied application of fan charts is in macroeconomic forecasting. The Bank of England’s Monetary Policy Committee pioneered the use of fan charts in 1997 to communicate probabilistic projections of inflation and GDP growth to policymakers, financial markets, and the public (Spiegelhalter et al., 2011). In these charts, the central interval represents 10% probability, and the largest interval 90% probability, calculated using a frequency interpretation of historical forecast errors (Spiegelhalter et al., 2011). The Bank’s fan charts have since become an international reference point for uncertainty communication in economic policy, and have been adapted by the UK Office for National Statistics to visualise both historical and forecasted uncertainties surrounding GDP growth (Galvão et al., 2019).

10.0.2 Strengths and Limitations

Strengths. Fan charts offer a number of genuine communicative advantages. For audiences with statistical training, they are highly informative, providing “the most effective” means of conveying uncertainty to “statisticians and other expert users with familiarity of probability distribution functions” (Galvão et al., 2019). The widening shape of the graphic acts as a powerful visual metaphor that communicates the structure of forecasting uncertainty intuitively and without requiring any statistical interpretation: the further out the forecast, the less certain it becomes. Bank of England (n.d.) argue that this makes fan charts an effective vehicle for conveying “the high degree of uncertainty in economic forecasts, especially at longer horizons, an important message for the public to hear.”

Fan charts also have a distinctive advantage in their ability to communicate the balance of risks around a central forecast. Because the shading can be applied asymmetrically, a “lopsided” fan chart visually communicates whether forecasters place more weight on outcomes falling above or below the central projection (Bank of England, n.d.). This depiction of distributional skewness — that the risks are tilted to the upside or the downside — is difficult to convey through intervals or point estimates alone, and is of direct relevance to policy makers and financial market participants who need to understand whether the risks around a forecast are symmetric or directional.

Limitations. Against these strengths must be set several well-documented limitations. The most significant is that the information density of fan charts “may be too complicated for many users” (Galvão et al., 2019). In expert surveys, professionals who were themselves comfortable with fan charts acknowledged concerns that “many journalists would not understand it and certainly would not pass it on to the public” (Galvão et al., 2019). For entry-level audiences, the simultaneous presentation of multiple nested probability bands, each requiring the viewer to hold in mind a different probability level, imposes a cognitive load that substantially exceeds what non-specialist users can typically manage.

A second limitation is the persistence of the deterministic construal error even in the presence of explicitly displayed uncertainty bands. Despite the visual prominence of the fan, end-users frequently ignore the uncertainty information entirely; as Galvão et al. (2019) observe, “much of the subsequent discussion” of Bank of England fan chart releases “lost sight of this and reverted to focus on the central estimate.” The widening bands, however visually striking, do not automatically redirect audience attention away from the single central line.

A third concern is the illusion of precision. When forecasters make subjective, ad hoc adjustments to the width or skew of the fan without grounding those adjustments in rigorous empirical modelling of historical forecast errors, the chart “conveys a false sense of precision” that may mislead users into believing the uncertainty bounds are more solidly founded than they actually are (Bank of England, n.d.).

Disagreement in the literature. There is a stark and unresolved division in the literature regarding the ultimate utility of fan charts. On one side, prominent practitioners and researchers praise them as an “innovative” and “pioneering approach” to communicating economic uncertainty that has advanced the field substantially (Galvão et al., 2019). On the other, a recent rigorous review of the Bank of England’s own practices reaches the blunt conclusion that fan charts “have outlived their usefulness” and “have weak conceptual foundations, convey little useful information over and above what could be communicated in other, more direct ways, and receive little attention from the public” (Bank of England, n.d.). The same review recommends that fan charts “should be eliminated” from institutional communications. A second source of tension concerns whether a numerical central estimate should accompany the fan chart. The Bank of England historically refused to include a specific central number alongside the graphic — precisely to prevent audiences from ignoring the bands and focusing solely on the point estimate — a practice that attracted sustained “criticism by journalists” who found the omission evasive (Spiegelhalter et al., 2011).

10.0.3 When to Use This Method

Fan charts are best deployed when communicating with statisticians, economists, and other expert users who are mathematically equipped to interpret complex probability distribution functions and directional skewness (Galvão et al., 2019). They are conceptually most appropriate when the primary goal is to demonstrate how “the range of uncertainty inherent in the forecasts broadens with the forecast horizon” (Bank of England, n.d.) — that is, when the time-varying structure of forecast uncertainty is itself the key message. In institutional contexts such as central bank communications, where the audience includes sophisticated financial market participants who trade on probabilistic assessments of policy paths, fan charts provide a level of information that simpler formats cannot match.

Fan charts are not appropriate for entry-level audiences without extensive interpretive support, and should not be used as a stand-alone communication tool even for informed audiences. The documented failure of non-experts and journalists to engage with the uncertainty bands suggests that fan charts function best as a supplementary display accompanying plain-language uncertainty summaries, rather than as the primary mode of uncertainty communication.

10.0.4 Good Practice

  • Ground the fan in empirical forecast errors: To avoid conveying a false sense of precision, ensure that the baseline widths of the fan are grounded in the historical record of forecast errors for the quantity in question, rather than in subjective, ad hoc committee adjustments (Bank of England, n.d.). Documenting and disclosing the methodological basis for the bands should be standard practice.
  • Complement with simpler formats: Because fan charts frequently overwhelm non-expert audiences, they should not be the sole mode of uncertainty communication. For entry-level and informed audiences, supplement or replace fan charts with plain-language risk statements, simple numerical ranges, or explicit narrative accounts of the balance of risk (Bank of England, n.d.; Galvão et al., 2019).
  • Consider providing a central estimate: The institutional reluctance to include a numerical central estimate alongside the fan — motivated by a desire to prevent audiences from ignoring the bands — may be counterproductive if it causes audiences to disengage from the graphic entirely. Providing the central estimate explicitly, with strong textual emphasis on the uncertainty bands, may better serve comprehension than withholding it (Spiegelhalter et al., 2011).
  • Label probability levels explicitly: Every shaded band in the fan chart should be clearly labelled with its associated probability level, either directly on the graphic or in an immediately adjacent legend. Audiences should not be required to consult a separate methodological document to understand what each band represents.
  • Test with the intended audience: Given the evidence that even professional users frequently revert to the central estimate, communication products using fan charts should be tested with target audiences before deployment to verify that the uncertainty information is actually being processed and used.
