5  Uncertainty and Related Concepts

The term “uncertainty” is frequently used as if it were interchangeable with a cluster of related concepts — risk, variability, randomness, and volatility — that in fact carry distinct technical meanings. This conflation is not merely an academic inconvenience; it has direct practical consequences for how information is communicated, how audiences interpret it, and how decisions are made. A communicator who presents variability data as if it were epistemic uncertainty, or who treats genuine uncertainty as if it were calculable risk, produces systematic distortions in audience understanding that no amount of presentational skill can fully correct. Conceptual clarity about the distinctions between these terms is therefore a prerequisite for effective communication.

As Makridakis & Bakas (2016) observe, “uncertainty” lacks a unique definition and is frequently treated as a linguistic catch-all, leading to theoretical confusion and flawed communication across disciplines. This section examines how uncertainty relates to and differs from four closely related concepts — risk, variability, randomness, and volatility — and concludes with a comparative summary table.

5.1 Uncertainty and Risk

The distinction between uncertainty and risk is foundational to the field and originates in the classic economic framework established by Frank Knight in 1921. In the Knightian paradigm, risk denotes a state where all potential outcomes are known and their precise probabilities can be mathematically calculated (Scoones, 2019). The probability that a fair die shows a six is exactly one in six; the probability that a bridge collapses under a specified load can be estimated from engineering models and empirical data. Risk, in this strict sense, is measurable, and the tools of classical probability theory are fully appropriate.

Uncertainty, by contrast, defines a state where probabilities cannot be reliably assigned because the underlying mechanisms are unknown, the environment is genuinely novel, or the outcomes themselves are contested (Scoones, 2019). Frank Knight’s original argument was that profit in a market economy arises precisely from this kind of genuine uncertainty — from situations where no competitor can calculate the odds. Stirling (n.d.b) formalises this distinction within the incertitude matrix discussed in Section 3.4: risk occupies the quadrant where both outcomes and likelihoods are unproblematic, while uncertainty in the strict sense describes conditions where likelihoods cannot be confidently estimated even though the space of outcomes may be known.

Despite the clarity of this theoretical distinction, it is routinely violated in practice. Stirling (n.d.a) documents a troubling institutional pattern in which regulatory and environmental bodies, facing political pressure to produce clear and actionable numbers, use the word “uncertainty” to describe situations where they have in fact asserted a specific probability — treating deep uncertainty as if it were calculable risk. This conflation is characterised as an irresponsible “pretence of knowledge” that forecloses deliberation about alternative possibilities and concentrates decision-making authority in technical hands. For communicators, the implication is that the choice of framing — presenting something as a quantified risk versus an unquantifiable uncertainty — carries substantial political and epistemic weight and should not be made carelessly.

5.2 Uncertainty and Variability

Variability refers to the natural, objective heterogeneity that exists within a population or a system — the genuine differences between individuals, locations, or time points that reflect real physical, biological, or social diversity (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). Human body weights vary; regional rainfall varies; the time it takes different people to respond to a drug varies. Variability is a property of the world, not of our knowledge of it.

Epistemic uncertainty, by contrast, refers to the subjective limits of a forecaster’s or analyst’s knowledge regarding a specific, single true value. As Bles et al. (2019) put it, epistemic uncertainty concerns “the past and present state of the world that arises because of what we do not know but could know in theory.” When a scientist reports uncertainty about the average global temperature in a given year, they are reporting epistemic uncertainty about a single quantity, not variability in that quantity.

The practical importance of this distinction lies in how audiences interpret graphical displays of uncertainty. The literature consistently documents that non-expert audiences routinely conflate the two, misinterpreting statistical distributions or error bars designed to show a forecaster’s lack of knowledge — epistemic uncertainty — as maps of physical population variation — variability (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). A patient shown a probability distribution over possible treatment outcomes may interpret it as showing how different patients respond, rather than how uncertain the physician is about this specific patient’s likely response. This misinterpretation can lead to systematically distorted risk perceptions and poorly calibrated decisions.

The communication implication is direct: whenever a display is intended to convey epistemic uncertainty about a single quantity, the accompanying text must explicitly clarify that the distribution represents a state of knowledge, not a description of population heterogeneity. As European Food Safety Authority et al. (2019) recommend, communicators should “explain clearly that the distribution represents uncertainty about the quantity of interest, for which there is a single true value.”

5.3 Uncertainty and Randomness

Randomness — what statisticians and machine learning researchers call aleatory uncertainty — describes the stochastic behaviour of a system that generates different outcomes even when initial conditions appear identical (Spiegelhalter, 2017). It is the irreducible variability of a chaotic or probabilistic process: the unpredictable fluctuations of a Brownian particle, the decay time of a radioactive atom, the outcome of a fair coin toss. Randomness is characterised by the fact that collecting more data or building a better model cannot, in principle, eliminate it — it reflects an inherent property of the system, not a limitation of the observer (Gruber et al., 2023).

Epistemic uncertainty, in contrast, represents a reducible gap in human knowledge: the uncertainty that arises because we do not know what the true value of a parameter is, which of several competing models is correct, or how a system will respond under novel conditions. In principle, additional information could resolve epistemic uncertainty; no additional information can resolve randomness.

This distinction has important practical implications. Communicating a source of uncertainty as “random” implies that it is irreducible and that further investment in data or modelling will not help. Communicating the same source as epistemic implies that it could, in principle, be reduced with more resources or better methods. As Gruber et al. (2023) point out, however, the boundary between randomness and epistemic uncertainty is not always as clear as textbook presentations suggest. A dice roll is conventionally treated as purely random; yet if one possessed a sufficiently detailed physical model of the dice, the throw, and the surface, the outcome would become perfectly predictable. The classification of uncertainty as aleatory or epistemic is therefore always relative to the current state of knowledge and the available models — not an absolute property of the phenomenon.

5.4 Uncertainty and Volatility

Volatility is the specific manifestation of aleatory uncertainty — randomness — within financial and economic time series. It refers to the degree of variance and the rapidity of fluctuations in market prices, exchange rates, or economic indicators over time (Eggen, 2025). Volatility is, in this sense, measurable: statistical models such as GARCH (Generalised Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity) exist precisely to estimate and forecast the conditional variance of financial returns, and market-implied volatility indices such as the VIX provide real-time quantitative measures of expected price variability (Makridakis & Bakas, 2016).

Volatility is therefore distinct from uncertainty in the strict Knightian sense. A highly volatile financial asset — one whose price fluctuates dramatically from day to day — may nonetheless operate within a risk framework if the statistical distribution of those fluctuations is well characterised and stable. The uncertainty that cannot be captured by standard volatility models is of a different character: it arises during structural breaks, crises, and Black Swan events, where the distributional assumptions that underpin the model are violated and the parameters of the volatility process itself change in ways that were not anticipated (Scoones, 2019). In such conditions, volatility modelling breaks down and the system moves from the domain of measurable risk into the domain of genuine uncertainty or ignorance.

Scoones (2019)’s discussion of the 2007–08 financial crisis illustrates this distinction vividly. Pre-crisis, the banking system operated in a world of apparent risk: volatility was being measured, hedged, and traded. The crisis revealed that beneath this veneer of quantified risk lay deep uncertainty about the structure of network dependencies, the reliability of rating agency assessments, and the behaviour of interconnected institutions under stress — uncertainties that no volatility model had captured or could have captured.

5.6 Disagreements in the Literature

Two disagreements are worth noting. The first concerns the institutional conflation of risk and uncertainty described above: Stirling (n.d.a) argues this is an active political choice with serious governance implications, while mainstream policy and regulatory practice treats it as a pragmatic necessity. The second concerns the status of the aleatory-epistemic boundary: Gruber et al. (2023) challenge the assumption that randomness and epistemic uncertainty are cleanly separable categories, arguing that the boundary is model-dependent and therefore always provisional. Both disagreements have practical consequences for communication: if the boundary between uncertainty and risk is political rather than technical, then choosing how to frame a situation has ethical dimensions that communicators should acknowledge; and if the boundary between randomness and epistemic uncertainty is provisional, then claiming that a source of uncertainty is irreducible should be treated with appropriate caution.

5.7 Good Practice

  • Explicitly distinguish uncertainty from variability in text: Because audiences routinely interpret distributional graphics as showing population variability rather than epistemic uncertainty, communicators must always provide explicit textual clarification that the display represents a state of knowledge about a single quantity (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Use subjective terminology for epistemic uncertainty: To prevent audiences from confusing expert epistemic judgements with population frequencies, use the term “percentage certainty” (e.g., “experts are 90% certain”) rather than “percentage probability” when describing subjective assessments, since laypeople associate “probability” with the physical frequency of random events (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Do not present deep uncertainty as risk: When probabilities cannot be reliably assigned, communicators should resist the institutional pressure to produce a number. Presenting deep uncertainty as if it were calculable risk is misleading and can be “irrational, unscientific and potentially misleading” (Stirling, n.d.a).
  • Acknowledge when volatility modelling reaches its limits: In financial and economic communication, be explicit about the conditions under which standard volatility models remain valid and the conditions — structural breaks, crises, genuinely novel events — under which they do not.
