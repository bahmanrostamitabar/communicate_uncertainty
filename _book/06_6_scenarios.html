14  Scenario and What-If Analysis

Scenario and what-if analyses are methods for exploring possible futures without sampling from an underlying probability distribution — making them fundamentally distinct from the frequency-based probabilistic approaches reviewed in earlier sections. Where prediction intervals, fan charts, and distributional displays express uncertainty as a probability assigned to different values of a continuous quantity, scenario analysis instead develops “a set of possible futures based on extrapolating current trends and varying key parameters, without sampling in a systematic manner from an uncertainty distribution” (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, 2015). The scenarios produced by this method are not random draws from a probability model; they are deliberately constructed, internally consistent narratives about how the future might unfold under different assumptions about the key drivers of change.

In strategic planning and organisational foresight, scenarios are described as “purposeful stories about how the contextual environment could unfold over time,” composed of internally consistent causal accounts of how different variables — social, technological, economic, political, and environmental — interact and co-evolve (Wright & Goodwin, 2009). Each scenario represents one coherent version of a possible future, designed not to be the most likely outcome but to be a plausible and internally consistent one that illuminates a specific combination of uncertainties. In algorithmic forecasting, what-if analysis takes a more specific and quantitative form: it is “a scenario modelling technique where you make a hypothetical change to a time series and compare the forecasts generated by these changes against the baseline” (Unknown Author, n.d.), allowing decision-makers to see directly how different choices or conditions would affect projected outcomes.

The shared feature of both approaches is their deliberate agnosticism about probability. By presenting a small number of distinctly different possible futures without attaching explicit probabilities to each, scenario analysis communicates the range of plausibility rather than the probability distribution of outcomes — a distinction that is both its greatest strength and the source of its most characteristic misuse.

14.0.1 Practical Example

Scenario and what-if analysis have been applied across a remarkably wide range of domains. In climate policy, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change uses scenarios — currently presented as Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs) — to represent different emission trajectories and model the Earth’s possible responses under each (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, 2015). The Royal Netherlands Meteorological Institute (KNMI) communicates climate risks to policy advisers and the public using a four-quadrant framework that intersects two key axes of uncertainty — low versus high CO2 emissions, and wetter versus drier climates — to produce four clearly differentiated scenarios for the future climate of the Netherlands (Pascoe et al., n.d.).

In public health, preparations for unknown future disease outbreaks are formalised through scenario or table-top exercises that envisage catastrophic possibilities, trace their implications through the health system, and test the robustness of response protocols against genuinely uncertain threats (Scoones, 2019). In social science research, Komp-Leukkunen (2020) applies scenario-based foresighting to map how the social integration of refugees might evolve under different hypothetical immigration policy frameworks — a domain where neither historical precedent nor probability models can adequately capture the range of possible futures.

In retail and commercial forecasting, algorithmic what-if analysis tools allow retailers to simulate the effect of specific business decisions — for example, examining how a 25% markdown on all clothing items, or an extension of a sale’s duration by two weeks, would affect baseline inventory and revenue projections relative to a no-change scenario (Unknown Author, n.d.).

14.0.2 Strengths and Limitations

Strengths. Scenario and what-if analysis offer three distinct advantages over probabilistic methods that make them the appropriate tool in specific circumstances.

The first and most fundamental is the ability to circumvent the problem of unquantifiable probability. Scenario analysis “offers an approach for handling situations of low predictability which avoids the requirement to make such estimates” (Wright & Goodwin, 2009) — that is, it enables rigorous planning under conditions of deep uncertainty where the assignment of reliable, calibrated probabilities is either impossible or would constitute a misleading pretence of precision. By constructing detailed, coherent possible futures without claiming to know their relative probabilities, scenario analysis is more epistemically honest than probabilistic methods in genuinely uncertain domains.

The second advantage is the capacity for collective sense-making and risk discovery. By building narrative storylines that trace the causal interactions between different drivers of change, scenario analysis “brings latent issues to the surface, so that it is more difficult to deny the prospect of high- impact events” (Wright & Goodwin, 2009). This social function of scenario planning is distinct from its analytical function: the process of constructing and discussing scenarios within a team or stakeholder group forces the explicit articulation of assumptions, surfaces hidden disagreements about how the world works, and makes it harder for high-consequence risks to be dismissed without engagement.

The third advantage is the promotion of adaptive strategy. By exploring divergent possible futures, scenario analysis encourages decision-makers to test the robustness of their plans across multiple eventualities, generating flexible strategies that remain effective across a range of possible outcomes rather than optimised for a single predicted scenario (Pascoe et al., n.d.). This is particularly valuable in long-horizon planning where structural changes in the system — demographic shifts, technological transitions, political realignments — are likely to render any single-point forecast obsolete.

Limitations. Against these genuine strengths, the literature documents several characteristic limitations and failure modes.

The most significant is deterministic misuse: analysts and end-users frequently forget that a scenario represents only one specific, internally consistent combination of variables, with a very low probability of exact occurrence. They instead use a single scenario as a deterministic forecast of what will happen, “without recognising that they have a low probability of occurrence” (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, 2015). This misuse is especially common when organisations select one scenario from a set and build all their planning around it, treating the selection as a prediction rather than as one plausible future among several.

A second limitation is future myopia — the tendency of scenario builders to generate futures that are variations on themes that participants already find familiar and plausible. Because scenarios are constructed using the existing mental models of the participants, they regularly fail to incorporate “unknown unknowns” or events without obvious, immediate causes (Wright & Goodwin, 2009). Participants in scenario workshops “tend to regularly emphasise economic factors” and other domains where they feel competent, systematically underweighting social, cultural, or technological disruptions that fall outside their professional frame.

A third limitation concerns the simulation heuristic and its paradoxical effects. While scenario planning is broadly designed to break decision-makers out of narrow historical framings, Wright & Goodwin (2009) identify a paradox: the act of actively constructing detailed causal chains can trigger the simulation heuristic, causing participants to become inappropriately overconfident in the specific scenario they have just built. Rather than opening up the space of possibilities, the construction process may reinforce existing framings and “produce increased, but inappropriate, confidence in one’s ability to anticipate the future” (Wright & Goodwin, 2009).

A fourth limitation applies specifically to algorithmic what-if analysis: when the hypothetical scenario involves conditions entirely outside the historical range of the training data, machine learning-based forecasting models may “not be accurate for novel scenarios where there is no reference available to determine whether a forecast is good” (Unknown Author, n.d.). The model’s ability to generate a number for any scenario should not be mistaken for its ability to generate a reliable number.

14.0.3 When to Use This Method

Scenario analysis is most appropriate under conditions of “deep uncertainty,” “ambiguity,” or “ignorance,” where fundamental outcomes are poorly defined, probabilities have a weak or non-existent empirical basis, and future predictability is low (Pascoe et al., n.d.). Stirling (n.d.) explicitly maps scenario analysis to the domain of ambiguity in his incertitude framework — situations where possible outcomes are contested or framed differently by different stakeholders — making it the most honest available approach when quantitative probabilities cannot be legitimately assigned.

It should be employed when the forecast horizon is sufficiently long that structural changes in the system — such as economic development trajectories, demographic shifts, or technological transitions — become the dominant drivers of outcomes (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, 2015). In such contexts, the scenario approach explicitly acknowledges what a probabilistic forecast would falsely conceal: that the future depends fundamentally on choices that have not yet been made.

Scenario analysis is less appropriate when the decision requires a specific probability estimate for risk management purposes, when the audience needs a single actionable threshold, or when the scenarios risk being misused as deterministic predictions.

14.0.4 Good Practice

  • Present an even number of scenarios: To prevent audiences from exhibiting a cognitive bias toward treating the “middle” scenario as the most likely, present an even number of scenarios — typically four — so that there is no natural central option to anchor on (Pascoe et al., n.d.).
  • Use backward logic to generate scenarios: To combat future myopia and generate genuinely diverse and challenging possibilities, work backwards from extreme objectives or failure states and deduce the causal drivers required to reach them, rather than extrapolating forward from the present (Wright & Goodwin, 2009). This “fault tree” approach is more likely to surface uncomfortable but important possibilities.
  • Explicitly state that scenarios are not predictions: Every scenario communication should include a clear, prominent statement that the scenarios represent possible futures, not forecasts of what will happen, and that they do not have assigned probabilities (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, 2015). This disclaimer should appear in the body of the communication, not merely in a methodological appendix.
  • Incorporate historical variability as a reference: When presenting scenarios to public or informed-level audiences, include graphical representations of historical year-to-year variability alongside the projected scenario range, to give audiences a tangible reference point for the magnitude of projected changes (Pascoe et al., n.d.).
  • Incorporate stakeholder motivations: Ensure that the behavioural responses embedded in scenarios are grounded in an understanding of what key stakeholders are likely to do when facing the conditions described in each scenario (Wright & Goodwin, 2009). Scenarios that do not account for how institutions and individuals will respond to the changes they describe are incomplete and may generate misleading strategic conclusions.
  • Combine with probabilistic methods where possible: In contexts where some quantitative basis for probability assignment exists, scenario analysis can be combined with probabilistic methods — for example, by assigning indicative probability ranges to each scenario or by using probabilistic models to explore the consequences of each scenario — producing a richer and more actionable uncertainty communication than either approach alone.
