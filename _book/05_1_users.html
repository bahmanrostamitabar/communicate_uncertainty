7  Audiences and Their Needs

Not all users of uncertain information need the same thing. One of the most important and consistently supported findings in the uncertainty communication literature is that different audiences have fundamentally different goals, different levels of statistical literacy, and different tolerances for complexity when engaging with uncertain information (National Research Council, 2006). A format that serves an expert statistician may actively mislead a general practitioner; a simplification appropriate for a newspaper headline may be insufficient for a policy analyst who needs to set regulatory thresholds. Designing uncertainty communication without first identifying the audience and their specific decision context is a recipe for poor outcomes regardless of the technical quality of the underlying analysis.

The most structured audience taxonomy in the reviewed literature is that developed by European Food Safety Authority et al. (2019), which divides audiences into three tiers based on their familiarity with scientific assessment, their knowledge of institutional processes, and the nature of the decision they need to make. This taxonomy provides a practical organising framework for matching communication methods to audiences, and its three levels — Entry, Informed, and Technical — are used throughout Chapter 9 to guide the “When to Use” recommendations for each communication approach.

7.0.1 Entry Level: General Public, Patients, and General Media

The entry-level audience encompasses individuals with “little or no familiarity with scientific assessment” and “little or no knowledge of institutional processes and relationships” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). This group includes members of the general public engaging with public health communications, patients making decisions about medical treatments, and consumers of mainstream news media covering scientific topics. Their engagement with uncertain information is typically brief, emotionally charged, and embedded in a specific decision context — whether to evacuate before a storm, whether to accept a medical intervention, whether to change behaviour in response to a public health recommendation.

For this audience, the communication goal is often simple behavioural signalling rather than full probabilistic comprehension. Spiegelhalter (2017) recommends providing a “top gist” — a clear, memorable summary of the most decision-relevant aspect of the uncertain information — before layering any additional detail. The evidence reviewed by Bles et al. (2020) suggests that communicating uncertainty to this audience using simple numerical ranges alongside a central estimate is the most robust approach: it does not substantially decrease trust in the source, and it avoids the ambiguity problems associated with verbal expressions. Complex distributional displays, fan charts, and full probability density functions are generally inappropriate for this audience and should be avoided.

7.0.2 Informed Level: Policymakers, Non-Governmental Organisations, and Specialist Journalists

The informed-level audience has “thorough knowledge of institutional processes and relationships” and “some or good understanding of scientific assessment but not advanced” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). This group includes policymakers and civil servants who use scientific evidence to inform regulatory decisions, NGO representatives engaged in evidence-based advocacy, and specialist journalists covering science, health, or economics beats. They engage with uncertain information more systematically than entry-level audiences, but they typically lack the statistical training to interpret complex distributional displays without guidance.

For this audience, plain-language summaries of uncertainty ranges, clearly defined categorical labels with explicit quantitative translations, and narrative accounts of the main sources and implications of uncertainty are most effective. European Food Safety Authority et al. (2019) recommend providing FAQs that directly address the most common misunderstandings, and explicitly defining any technical terms that appear in the communication. The fundamental tension between sender and receiver preferences documented by Dhami & Mandel (2022) is particularly acute for this audience: policymakers consistently prefer precise numerical uncertainty information when making consequential decisions, even though the scientists who advise them frequently default to vague verbal expressions that offer face-saving flexibility.

7.0.3 Technical Level: Domain Experts, Statisticians, and Risk Managers

The technical-level audience possesses “advanced understanding of scientific assessment, use of data, tools and methodologies” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). This group includes domain scientists, statisticians, quantitative risk analysts, and professional risk managers who use uncertainty information as an input to further modelling, regulatory analysis, or portfolio management. They are equipped to interpret complex probabilistic displays, to interrogate the assumptions underlying uncertainty estimates, and to propagate uncertainty through subsequent analyses.

For this audience, full probability distributions, fan charts, prediction intervals at multiple levels, and detailed documentation of model assumptions are not only appropriate but necessary. Raftery (2016) identifies a specific sub-type of technical user — the “decision theorist” — who possesses an explicit loss function and needs the full predictive distribution to formally minimise expected loss. This is the only user type for whom the full distributional complexity of a probabilistic forecast is genuinely required, and even here, the practical challenges of eliciting and applying a formal loss function mean that simpler quantile-based summaries often suffice (Raftery, 2016).

It is worth noting, as Galvão et al. (2019) observe in the context of fan charts for economic forecasting, that even expert users are not immune to the deterministic construal error — the tendency to ignore uncertainty bands and focus on the central estimate. Technical communication should therefore still include explicit prompts to engage with the full range of uncertainty, not merely present it and assume it will be used.

7.1 Disagreements in the Literature

Two significant tensions run through the literature on why and to whom uncertainty should be communicated, and both have direct implications for practice.

The first is what might be called the uncertainty paradox. On one hand, robust experimental evidence from Bles et al. (2020) demonstrates that the provision of numerical uncertainty — particularly as a numeric range — does not substantially decrease public trust in either the numbers or the source. This is reassuring for communicators who worry that expressing uncertainty will undermine their credibility. On the other hand, Leffrang & Müller (2025) find that highly effective uncertainty visualisations — specifically ensemble spaghetti plots that accurately depict extreme volatility — can cause individuals to become “alienated and less likely to take the advice,” actively reducing their trust in algorithmic guidance. The apparent contradiction between these findings suggests that the relationship between uncertainty communication and trust is not linear: modest, well-calibrated uncertainty communication preserves or even enhances trust, while overwhelming visualisations of volatility and extreme scenarios may cross a threshold beyond which the audience disengages. The implication for practice is that communicators should aim for appropriate honesty about uncertainty, not maximal transparency at any cost.

The second tension concerns the persistent conflict between sender and receiver preferences. Dhami & Mandel (2022) document that senders — the scientists, forecasters, and analysts who generate uncertain information — strongly prefer to express that uncertainty in vague, verbal terms, because doing so provides “face-saving” flexibility and reduces their accountability if a prediction turns out to be wrong. Receivers — the policymakers, public, and patients who must act on the information — strongly prefer precise, numerical formats, because they need concrete boundaries to make consequential decisions. This mismatch is not merely a matter of different tastes; it has direct consequences for decision quality. When communicators default to vague verbal expressions for self-protective reasons, they systematically deprive their audiences of the information those audiences need most. Dhami & Mandel (2022) conclude that “when it really matters, both senders and receivers prefer the precise numeric expression of uncertainty” — a finding that suggests the sender’s preference for vagueness may itself reflect a form of motivated reasoning rather than a genuine assessment of what serves the audience.

7.2 Good Practice

  • Determine decision relevance first: Before designing an uncertainty communication, identify the specific decision the audience is trying to make. Fischhoff & Davis (2014) recommend assessing whether the audience is “looking for a signal, choosing among fixed options, or learning to create options,” and tailoring the depth and format of the uncertainty information to that specific decision context.
  • Segment the audience: Never assume a monolithic audience. Practitioners must segment audiences into target groups and identify their needs, beliefs, and statistical skills before developing communication materials (Spiegelhalter, 2017). Different groups within the same communication context — for example, specialist journalists and general readers of the same news outlet — may require fundamentally different treatments of the same uncertainty information.
  • Layer the information: To satisfy multiple audiences simultaneously, employ a tiered or layered communication approach. Provide a clear top-line gist for entry-level audiences, followed by progressively more complex layers — numerical intervals, source explanations, and technical appendices — for informed and technical users (Spiegelhalter, 2017).
  • Develop, test, and evaluate: Communication products should not be released on the assumption that they will be understood as intended. Spiegelhalter (2017) recommend developing materials with target groups, testing them for comprehension, and evaluating their effects on decision-making before wide deployment.
  • Resist the face-saving temptation: Communicators should resist the institutional and personal incentives to suppress or vague-ify uncertainty for reasons of self-protection. The evidence reviewed in this section suggests that honest, calibrated uncertainty communication preserves institutional trust over the long run, while suppression of uncertainty creates fragility that is revealed when events diverge from deterministic predictions.
