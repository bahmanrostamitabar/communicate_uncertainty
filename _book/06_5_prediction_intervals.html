14  Prediction Intervals and Quantiles

Prediction intervals and quantiles represent uncertainty by identifying specific points or bounded ranges in the probability distribution of a future outcome — communicating not the full shape of the distribution but the most decision-relevant summaries of its extent. A prediction interval specifies a range within which a future observation is expected to fall with a stated probability: a 90% prediction interval, for instance, is constructed to contain the true future value on approximately 90% of occasions when the forecast is made under similar conditions (Raftery, 2016). A quantile specifies a threshold below which a given proportion of possible outcomes are expected to fall: a forecast at the 0.65 quantile, for example, “will estimate a value that is lower than the observed value 65% of the time” (Unknown Author, n.d.).

These two closely related concepts can be presented visually — most commonly through error bars, box plots, or shaded bands on time series plots — or numerically, as explicit lower and upper bounds accompanying a central estimate. In a standard box plot, a central rectangle represents the middle quartiles (the 25th to 75th percentile range), a line inside the box indicates the median, and extending whiskers represent upper and lower probability bounds (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). In time series forecasting, prediction intervals are typically displayed as a shaded region surrounding the central forecast line, widening as the horizon extends to reflect the accumulation of uncertainty over time.

The relationship between prediction intervals and the full distributional displays described in Chapter 15 is one of summarisation: a prediction interval extracts two specific quantiles from the full distribution and presents them without the distributional shape that lies between and beyond them. This compression is precisely what makes intervals practical for non-expert audiences — and precisely what creates their characteristic limitations, as the information that is discarded by the compression is not always negligible.

14.0.1 Practical Example

Prediction intervals have been applied across a wide range of forecasting contexts. In weather forecasting, the University of Washington’s Probcast website communicated temperature uncertainty to the general public using the 10th and 90th percentiles of the forecast distribution — an 80% prediction interval — alongside the probability of specific adverse weather events (Raftery, 2016). The choice of 80% rather than 95% intervals was deliberate: while 95% intervals contain more true outcomes, they are “also much wider and hence may be judged less useful” for practical decision-making by users planning outdoor activities or making weather-sensitive choices (Raftery, 2016).

In epidemiological forecasting during the COVID-19 pandemic, 95% prediction intervals were used to present probabilistic bounds around time-series forecasts of hospital bed occupancy, providing health system managers with an upper bound for capacity planning (Leffrang & Müller, 2025). In demographic forecasting, Raftery & Ševčíková (2023) use prediction intervals around Total Fertility Rate projections to demonstrate how forecast uncertainty is “narrow for short-term predictions, then widens and finally stabilises as the forecast horizon increases” — a pattern that is directly legible from the interval display without requiring the viewer to interpret a full distributional shape.

In intelligence analysis, Mandel & Irwin (2021) advocate for replacing vague linguistic probability terms with explicit numeric credible intervals: reporting that there is a “65% to 85% probability that a given event will occur” is more precise, more verifiable, and less susceptible to motivated misinterpretation than verbal alternatives such as “likely.”

In retail and supply chain management, the quantile framework is applied directly to asymmetric cost structures: when “the cost of being understocked is often higher than the cost of being overstocked,” planning at the 75th quantile rather than the median is more economically appropriate, because it provides protection against the higher-cost error at an acceptable frequency (Unknown Author, n.d.).

14.0.2 Strengths and Limitations

Strengths. Prediction intervals have several important practical advantages. First, they facilitate risk management under conditions of asymmetric costs. Because quantiles can be specified to match the cost structure of a particular decision — providing more protection against the more costly type of error — they enable decision-makers to optimise their planning in a way that a simple central estimate cannot support (Unknown Author, n.d.). The “risk avoider” identified by Raftery (2016) — a user whose primary goal is to limit the probability of an adverse outcome below an acceptable threshold — finds a specific quantile far more actionable than the full distributional information from which it is derived.

Second, visualising uncertainty through box plots and error bars enables users to extract mean estimates and make subsequent decisions with higher accuracy than complex distributional displays. Edwards et al. (2012) found that “error bar displays and box plots work best for mean estimation and choice of action” — a finding that supports the use of intervals precisely in contexts where simplicity and actionability are priorities.

Third, communicating uncertainty through numerical ranges with a point estimate does not significantly reduce public trust in the source. Bles et al. (2020) demonstrate that this format “does not substantially decrease trust in either the numbers or the source” — directly addressing one of the most common concerns among institutional communicators who worry that expressing uncertainty will damage their credibility.

Fourth, quantile-based communication offers a practical solution to the challenge of expert knowledge communication. Raftery (2016) demonstrate that a single, well-chosen quantile can serve as the entire actionable output of a complex probabilistic analysis — as in the International Whaling Commission’s use of the fifth percentile of the bowhead whale replacement yield distribution as a precautionary quota limit — delivering probabilistically principled decisions without requiring the audience to interpret a full distribution.

Limitations. The most persistent and well-documented limitation of prediction intervals is what Toet et al. (2016) call the uniform distribution fallacy: when numeric intervals or error bars are presented without explicit information about distributional shape, audiences routinely assume a uniform distribution, incorrectly believing that all values within the range are equally probable. As Spiegelhalter et al. (2011) describe it, the visual presentation of an interval makes it appear as though “values within the interval are equally likely,” encouraging users to anchor on the extreme bounds rather than the central estimate — even though those extremes represent the least likely outcomes within the interval.

A related limitation is excessive focus on the extremes. Because the bounds of an interval are visually salient — they are the edges of a bar or the endpoints of a whisker — users tend to anchor on them, producing a systematic bias toward the consideration of extreme scenarios at the expense of the more probable central outcomes (Spiegelhalter et al., 2011).

A third limitation is the deterministic construal error in its interval- specific form: audiences frequently interpret the boundaries of a prediction interval as strict categorical partitions, perceiving values within the interval as “accurate” and values outside it as “erroneous” — as if the interval were a binary classifier rather than a probabilistic statement (Leffrang & Müller, 2025). This misinterpretation can lead to inappropriate surprise and loss of trust when an actual outcome falls outside a stated interval, even if that outcome had a non-trivial probability.

Finally, because box plots and ranges are also widely used to display population variability, audiences routinely misinterpret epistemic uncertainty intervals as descriptions of real-world heterogeneity — the same confusion documented for distributional displays in Chapter 15.

Disagreement in the literature. The literature reveals a historical disagreement about how numeric intervals affect perceived competence and trust. Bles et al. (2020) conclude from meta-analysis that providing numeric ranges does not negatively affect trust, while earlier research by Johnson and Slovic (referenced in Bles et al. (2020)) found that interval communication “signalled honesty and competency for some participants, but dishonesty and incompetency for others.” A second tension concerns the visual design of interval boundaries: while traditional statistical graphics use sharp, crisp boundary lines for intervals, Toet et al. (2016) argue that “blurred or dashed (versus sharp) borders” would better convey that values become gradually less probable as they approach the extremes of the interval, reducing the deterministic construal error. This recommendation has not yet been widely adopted in practice.

14.0.3 When to Use This Method

Prediction intervals and quantile forecasts are appropriate across a wide range of audiences and contexts, making them among the most versatile methods in the uncertainty communicator’s toolkit. For risk avoiders who need to guarantee an outcome up to a specific probability threshold, a specific quantile — chosen to match the cost structure of the decision — is the appropriate output (Raftery, 2016). For mass-media communications regarding scientific facts, numerical intervals with a central point estimate represent “the most robust format for conveying uncertainty without confusing the general public” (Bles et al., 2020).

The choice of coverage probability matters. Raftery (2016) recommend 80% prediction intervals over the conventional 95% for public-facing communications, on the grounds that narrower intervals are more useful for practical decision-making even though they contain fewer actual outcomes. For high-stakes decisions where the tail risk is the primary concern — flood protection, capacity planning, infrastructure resilience — wider intervals or specific extreme quantiles may be more appropriate.

Intervals should be preferred over full distributional displays when the audience is at the entry or informed level, when the communication context does not support the cognitive load of distributional interpretation, or when the specific decision can be mapped onto a single probability threshold.

14.0.4 Good Practice

  • Always state the associated probability explicitly: Never present a numeric range in isolation. Always pair the range with a clear statement of the probability that the quantity falls within it — for example, “there is a 90% probability that the outcome will fall between X and Y” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Always provide the central estimate: Accompany every interval with a median or mean estimate to counter the tendency of users to focus on the extremes and to signal whether the distribution is symmetric or skewed (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Incorporate density cues where possible: To reduce the uniform distribution fallacy, supplement intervals or box plots with a visual density strip or explicit text explaining that central values are more probable than extreme values (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Consider blurred or dashed interval borders: To reduce the deterministic construal error, explore the use of visual treatments — such as gradual fading at the interval boundaries — that signal the continuous nature of the probability distribution beyond the stated bounds (Toet et al., 2016).
  • Communicate interval bounds explicitly in text: Do not require audiences to read the bounds of an interval from a graphical axis. Always state the upper and lower bounds numerically in accompanying text so that the precise values are unambiguous (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Always accompany box plots with explanations: Because box plots are frequently misunderstood, include a textual or graphical key that explicitly defines what the whiskers, box, and central line represent, and reiterate that the graphic represents epistemic uncertainty about a single quantity rather than population variability (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Match the quantile to the decision: When advising specific users rather than a general audience, choose the quantile to communicate based on the cost structure of their decision — protecting against the high-cost error at an appropriate frequency rather than defaulting to the conventional 50th or 95th percentile (Raftery, 2016; Unknown Author, n.d.).
