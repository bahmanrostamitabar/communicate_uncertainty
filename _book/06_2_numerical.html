11  Numerical Expressions of Uncertainty

Numerical expressions of uncertainty convey the likelihood of an outcome or the margin of error surrounding an estimate through explicit quantitative values — moving beyond the inherent ambiguity of natural language to provide mathematically scalable, verifiable, and aggregable representations of probability. These expressions can take a wide variety of forms: precise point probabilities (a 75% chance), imprecise numeric ranges (a 65–85% chance), fractions (3 in 4), odds (3 to 1 in favour), or natural frequencies such as “1 in 10” or “15 out of 100” (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). By mapping uncertainty onto a 0 to 1 (or 0% to 100%) scale, numerical expressions allow degrees of certainty to be quantified, compared, combined, and communicated with a precision that verbal expressions cannot achieve. As Mandel & Irwin (2021) observe, “unlike linguistic probabilities, numeric probabilities have a transparent ordering that users agree on” — a property that is fundamental to their value in decision-making contexts where different sources of uncertain information must be integrated.

Numerical expressions are not a single format but a family of closely related formats, each with distinctive perceptual and cognitive properties. Percentages express probability as a proportion of 100; frequencies express it as a count out of a specified denominator; fractions express it as a ratio of numerator to denominator; odds express it as the ratio of favourable to unfavourable outcomes; and decimal probabilities express it as a number between 0 and 1. These formats are mathematically equivalent — a 10% probability, a 1-in-10 frequency, a 0.10 decimal, and 1-to-9 odds all describe the same underlying quantity — yet the evidence reviewed below demonstrates that they can produce substantially different perceptions, decisions, and levels of comprehension in the same audience. The choice of numerical format is therefore not merely a matter of convention; it is a consequential communication decision.

11.0.1 Practical Example

Numerical expressions of uncertainty are deployed across virtually every domain of quantitative science and risk communication. In meteorology, weather forecasters express the Probability of Precipitation as a percentage — “a 30 percent chance of rain” — in public forecasts that reach millions of people daily (National Research Council, 2006). In intelligence analysis, Mandel & Irwin (2021) advocate replacing vague verbal expressions with numerical credible intervals — for example, reporting a “65% to 85% probability that a given event will occur” — to provide unambiguous, verifiable communication of analytic confidence.

In public health and epidemiology, medical risk calculators communicate chronic disease risk using frequency formats: “12 of 100 people with this level of risk will have a heart attack in the next 10 years” (Spiegelhalter, 2017). This frequency format is specifically chosen because it grounds the abstract probability in a concrete, imaginable group of people rather than an abstract proportion. In food safety, communicators express incremental risks using absolute frequency differences: “one extra case of bowel cancer expected in every 100 people eating processed meat daily” (Spiegelhalter, 2017). This absolute risk format, as discussed further below, is preferred over relative risk expressions precisely because it conveys the actual magnitude of the risk increment without the inflation that relative framing introduces.

11.0.2 Strengths and Limitations

Strengths. Numerical expressions offer four distinctive advantages over verbal alternatives that make them the preferred format for high-stakes uncertainty communication.

The first is unambiguous ordering and transparency. Unlike verbal expressions, which can be interpreted across a 60-percentage-point range for a single term, numerical probabilities have a “transparent ordering that users agree on” (Mandel & Irwin, 2021). A stated probability of 35% is unambiguously lower than one of 60%; there is no analogous consensus about whether “possible” is higher or lower than “maybe.” This ordering property is essential for comparative risk communication, where audiences need to weigh the probability of one outcome against another.

The second advantage is enhanced information processing and aggregation. When decision-making requires the integration of uncertainty information from multiple sources, numerical formats substantially outperform verbal ones. “Arithmetic accuracy and logical coherence of averaging and multiplying probabilities are lower when these are expressed verbally than when expressed as point numeric values” (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). In settings where risk information must be combined — for example, when a patient needs to weigh the probability of benefit against the probability of harm from a treatment — numerical expressions produce more coherent and better-calibrated judgements.

The third advantage is credibility preservation. Communicating uncertainty using numerical ranges or point estimates does not substantially reduce public trust in the source or the numbers themselves (Bles et al., 2020). Senders who make an inaccurate low-probability forecast also suffer less credibility loss if they used a numerical expression than if they used an outcome-incongruent verbal term (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). This evidence directly challenges the common assumption among institutional communicators that expressing numerical uncertainty will damage their credibility.

The fourth advantage is receiver preference for consequential decisions. While senders prefer verbal expressions for their face-saving properties, receivers consistently and strongly prefer numerical precision when they must make high-stakes decisions (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). Crucially, this preference for numbers is not confined to high-numeracy individuals: “when it really matters, both senders and receivers prefer the precise numeric expression of uncertainty” (Dhami & Mandel, 2022).

Limitations. Despite these advantages, numerical expressions are subject to several well-documented biases and limitations that communicators must actively address.

The first is numeracy constraints. A significant proportion of the general public lacks the mathematical training necessary to intuitively process quantitative probability information, which limits the efficacy of purely numerical communication with entry-level audiences (National Research Council, 2006). However, as Dhami & Mandel (2022) note, recent evidence suggests that low-numeracy individuals are not actually harmed by numerical expressions relative to verbal ones — they do not make worse decisions with numbers than with words — and they judge communicators more harshly when vague verbal formats appear to mask errors. The assumption that numbers should be avoided for low-numeracy audiences may therefore be overstated.

The second limitation is reference class ambiguity. Numerical probabilities frequently fail to communicate clearly because audiences misunderstand the implicit denominator. A 60% chance of rain is routinely misinterpreted as meaning rain for 60% of the day’s duration, or rain over 60% of the forecast area, rather than rain on 60% of days with similar meteorological conditions (Spiegelhalter, 2017). This ambiguity is not a property of the number itself but of the failure to specify the reference class — the population of events to which the probability applies.

The third limitation is ratio bias and denominator neglect. When numerical uncertainties are expressed as fractions, human probability perception is heavily biased by the numerator. People incorrectly perceive a risk of 1,286 out of 10,000 as larger than one of 24.14 out of 100, because the absolute size of the numerator (1,286) is larger (Spiegelhalter, 2017). In extreme cases, audiences exhibit “denominator neglect” — focusing entirely on the number of adverse events and ignoring the size of the population from which it is drawn.

The fourth limitation is the 1-in-X magnification effect. Expressing probabilities in a “1-in-X” ratio format systematically inflates the audience’s perception of risk compared to numerically equivalent “N-in-X” formats (Ripberger et al., 2022). A “1 in 33 chance” is perceived as more alarming than a “3 in 100 chance” even though they are mathematically identical.

The fifth limitation is relative risk inflation. Presenting numerical uncertainty as a relative risk reduction — for example, “a 50% decrease in the risk of heart attack” — yields larger, more persuasive numbers than presenting the same information as an absolute risk difference — “a decrease from 8% to 4%” (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). Relative risk framing causes audiences to substantially overestimate the magnitude of the effect, with direct consequences for medical and policy decision-making.

Disagreement in the literature. The most significant and unresolved disagreement in the numerical expressions literature concerns the choice between percentage and frequency formats. Some researchers — notably Gigerenzer and Hoffrage — argue strongly that natural frequencies vastly improve comprehension and Bayesian reasoning, because they align with human experiential learning about counts of events rather than abstract proportions (Spiegelhalter, 2017). A substantial body of research has been developed supporting the frequency advantage. However, contradictory empirical results from Joslyn and Nichols demonstrate that non-expert users actually understood weather forecast uncertainty better when probabilities were presented as percentages rather than frequencies, directly contradicting the frequency advantage hypothesis (Raftery, 2016). Extensive subsequent reviews conclude that there is “no consistent benefit” to either format and that performance depends heavily on the specific task, the population tested, and the context of the communication (Ripberger et al., 2022). This disagreement remains unresolved and argues for empirical testing in specific contexts rather than universal format prescriptions.

11.0.3 When to Use This Method

Numerical expressions should be used as the primary mode of communication whenever the objective is the unambiguous communication of probability to support consequential decision-making (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). They are appropriate across all three audience levels described in Chapter 7, though the specific format chosen — percentage, frequency, odds, or range — should be adapted to the audience’s familiarity and the nature of the decision.

Numerical formats are especially important when the audience is expected to combine uncertainty information from multiple sources, to compare probabilities of different outcomes, or to make a decision that requires trading off asymmetric costs and benefits (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). In these contexts, the cognitive advantages of numerical over verbal formats are most pronounced and most consequential.

Numerical expressions are less appropriate as the primary format when the underlying uncertainty is genuinely unquantifiable — for example, when expressing the pedigree of evidence or the degree of scientific consensus — in which case categorical approaches or verbal expressions of meta-uncertainty are more honest, as discussed in Chapter 12 and Chapter 10.

11.0.4 Good Practice

  • Use absolute rather than relative risks: To prevent biased, inflated threat perceptions, always express risks and risk changes in absolute rather than relative terms. Present the actual probabilities before and after an intervention, not just the percentage reduction (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019). If relative risks are reported at all, always accompany them with the absolute risk difference.
  • Fix the denominator: When making risk comparisons using frequency formats, always keep the denominator constant across the comparisons — for example, “5 out of 1,000” versus “20 out of 1,000” — rather than allowing the denominator to vary across comparisons (Spiegelhalter, 2017). Varying denominators activate ratio bias and make comparisons unreliable.
  • Avoid “1-in-X” formats: The “1-in-X” format reliably inflates risk perception and should be replaced by “N-in-X” formats using a consistent base-10 denominator — for example, “3 in 100” rather than “1 in 33” (Ripberger et al., 2022).
  • Explicitly define the reference class: Every numerical probability must be accompanied by a precise specification of what population, time horizon, and conditions it refers to. “A 30% chance of rain” should be accompanied by a clear statement of what “30%” means in the specific forecasting context (Spiegelhalter, 2017).
  • Use “% certainty” rather than “% probability” for subjective judgements: Because laypeople associate “probability” with the physical frequency of random events, communicators expressing subjective expert judgements should use terms such as “experts are 90% certain” rather than “there is a 90% probability,” to clarify that the number represents a state of knowledge rather than a population frequency (European Food Safety Authority et al., 2019).
  • Test the chosen format empirically: Given the unresolved frequency- versus-percentage debate and the strong context-dependence of numerical format effects, the appropriate numerical format should be determined through empirical testing with the target audience rather than on the basis of general recommendations (Ripberger et al., 2022).
  • Provide multiple formats for different audiences: When the same numerical uncertainty must be communicated to audiences with different backgrounds, consider providing the information in multiple formats simultaneously — for example, “10% — that is, 1 in 10” — to maximise the probability that at least one format is well matched to each reader’s prior experience.
