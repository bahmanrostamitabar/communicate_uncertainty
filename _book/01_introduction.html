2  Introduction

Uncertainty is an inescapable feature of knowledge, decision-making, and communication across virtually every domain of human endeavour. A physician advising a patient on the likely progression of a disease, a meteorologist issuing a five-day forecast, an economist projecting next year’s growth, a hydrologist modelling flood risk, an intelligence analyst assessing the likelihood of a geopolitical event — all are working with incomplete, imperfect, or inherently variable information. In each case, how that uncertainty is understood, framed, and communicated to those who must act on it can have consequences as significant as the underlying knowledge itself.

Yet despite its ubiquity, uncertainty is frequently treated as an inconvenience to be minimised or hidden rather than a property of knowledge to be honestly represented. Point estimates are reported without ranges. Forecasts are presented as predictions. Probabilistic language is avoided for fear of appearing indecisive or undermining confidence. The result is a systematic mismatch between what is actually known and what is communicated — one that can mislead decision-makers, erode public trust, and contribute to poor outcomes. As Fischhoff & Davis (2014) argue, unless uncertainty is communicated effectively, decision makers may place too much or too little faith in the information they receive, with potentially serious consequences. The principle is stated simply by National Research Council (2006): no forecast is complete without a description of its uncertainty.

The challenge is compounded by a fundamental tension identified across the literature. Those who generate uncertain information — forecasters, scientists, analysts — frequently prefer to communicate it in vague, qualitative terms, partly because doing so reduces their accountability if a prediction turns out to be wrong (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). Those who consume uncertain information — the public, policymakers, patients, risk managers — typically prefer precise, numerical communication, because they need concrete boundaries to make high-stakes decisions (Dhami & Mandel, 2022). This sender-receiver mismatch is one of the most persistent and practically consequential problems in the field.

Communicating uncertainty is also genuinely difficult. Audiences differ in their statistical literacy, their tolerance for ambiguity, and their preferences for how information is presented. Methods that work well for expert audiences — such as probability distributions or quantile intervals — may confuse or alienate non-specialist readers. Simpler formats, such as categorical ratings or verbal expressions, risk oversimplifying or introducing their own forms of misunderstanding. There is no universally best method; what works depends on the message, the medium, and the audience. Furthermore, as Bles et al. (2019) demonstrate, uncertainty lacks a single agreed definition across disciplines, complicating both the production and reception of uncertainty communication at a fundamental level.

This paper aims to serve as a practical, cross-disciplinary reference for anyone who needs to communicate uncertainty — whether in a scientific report, a policy document, a data visualisation, a public health bulletin, or a financial forecast. It is written to be accessible to readers from any discipline, and draws on literature spanning statistics, meteorology, climate science, economics, medicine, psychology, intelligence analysis, ecology, and risk communication. The paper does not attempt to survey the full literature on uncertainty quantification, probabilistic modelling, or decision theory — each of these is a vast field in its own right. The focus is squarely on communication: how uncertain information is represented and conveyed to an audience, and how different representational choices affect understanding, trust, and decision-making.

2.1 Structure of This Paper

The paper is organised as follows. Chapter 3 introduces uncertainty as a concept, reviewing key definitions and frameworks from across the literature, including the distinction between epistemic and aleatory uncertainty, Stirling’s four-dimensional matrix of incertitude, and the concepts of deep uncertainty and unknown unknowns. Chapter 4 examines where uncertainty comes from, identifying five major categories of source: measurement and data uncertainty, model uncertainty, parameter uncertainty, structural and scenario uncertainty, and human and judgmental uncertainty. Chapter 5 situates uncertainty among four closely related concepts — variability, volatility, randomness, and risk — clarifying how these differ and why the distinctions matter for communication. Chapter 6 asks why uncertainty should be communicated at all, examines the consequences of failing to do so, and considers the range of audiences who may need to engage with uncertain information, from technical experts to the general public. The core of the paper is Chapter 8, which reviews eight distinct approaches to communicating uncertainty: forecast trajectories, fan charts, probability distributions, prediction intervals and quantiles, categorical approaches, scenario and what-if analysis, verbal expressions, and numerical expressions. For each method, the paper discusses what it is, provides a practical example with a figure placeholder, examines its strengths and limitations, identifies when it is most appropriate, and offers good practice recommendations. Finally, Chapter 17 draws together cross-cutting principles, presents a summary table matching methods to audience types, and identifies open questions for future work.

2.2 How to Use This Paper

This paper is designed to be read as a whole, but it can equally be used as a reference in which individual sections are consulted as needed. Readers who are new to the topic may wish to read Sections 3 through 6 before turning to the methods. Practitioners who already have a working understanding of uncertainty and are seeking guidance on communication methods may proceed directly to Chapter 8. Each method subsection in Chapter 8 is self-contained, with cross-references to the conceptual sections where relevant.

Note

A note on scope: This paper focuses on the communication of quantitative or semi-quantitative uncertainty — uncertainty that can be expressed, at least in part, through numbers, ranges, probabilities, or calibrated language. The communication of purely qualitative uncertainty, philosophical scepticism, and the sociological and political dimensions of uncertainty governance, while touched upon where relevant, are not the primary focus.
