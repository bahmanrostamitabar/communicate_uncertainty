4  Sources of Uncertainty

Understanding where uncertainty comes from is as important as understanding what it is. In practice, uncertainty does not emerge from a single point in the analytical process but cascades throughout the entire pipeline — from the initial collection of data, through the construction and calibration of models, to the interpretation and communication of results. As Bles et al. (2019) note, no consensus has emerged as to a general framework for categorising these sources, perhaps because the wide variety of contexts and tasks being considered makes any single taxonomy difficult to apply universally. For the purposes of this review, sources of uncertainty are grouped into five broad categories: measurement and data uncertainty, model uncertainty, parameter uncertainty, structural and scenario uncertainty, and human and judgmental uncertainty. These categories are not mutually exclusive — in practice, multiple sources interact and compound one another — but distinguishing them is essential for designing appropriate communication strategies.

Before choosing how to communicate uncertainty, communicators should ask a prior question: why does the uncertainty exist? Is it because of unavoidable natural variation, the difficulties of measurement, limited knowledge of underlying processes, or disagreement between experts? (Bles et al., 2019). The answer shapes both the appropriate method of communication and the degree of honesty required about the limits of what is known.

4.1 Measurement and Data Uncertainty

At the most foundational level, uncertainty is rooted in the physical and practical limits of observation, measurement, and data collection. No instrument is perfectly precise; no sampling procedure captures a population without error; no data set is complete. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change identifies data uncertainty as arising from “missing, inaccurate or non-representative data, inappropriate spatial or temporal resolution” (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, n.d.). In algorithmic and statistical forecasting, the specific concerns include omitted variables, measurement errors, and data that violate the assumptions of independence and identical distribution that underpin most standard models (Gruber et al., 2023).

Qualitative research with natural hazard scientists captures this source of uncertainty in vivid terms: a primary driver of uncertainty is simply “not having all the information or not having the ability to get the information,” leading to critical gaps in the empirical record that must either be filled with assumptions or acknowledged as unknowns (Doyle et al., 2023). The consequences of data gaps are often underappreciated precisely because they are invisible: by the time a dataset is analysed, its origins, incompleteness, and imperfections have typically been forgotten or omitted from the report.

Data uncertainty is also temporally dynamic rather than fixed. In macroeconomic statistics, for example, initial data estimates are subject to ongoing revision as statistical agencies succeed in incorporating additional information over subsequent months and years (Galvão et al., 2019). This means that the uncertainty associated with a GDP figure, for instance, is not a static quantity at the time of release but changes as the data vintage evolves — a dimension of uncertainty that is rarely communicated to users of economic statistics.

4.2 Model Uncertainty

Even with perfect data, our models of the world are inevitably simplifications. Every model embeds assumptions about which variables matter, how they relate to one another, what functional forms govern those relationships, and how the system behaves under conditions that may differ from those in which the model was calibrated. As Mehta & Srivastava (2020) observe, “models are simplified representations of complex systems, and as such are never the ‘real’ thing.” Model uncertainty specifically encompasses the variation in outputs that occurs because “different models simulate different responses” to the same inputs — a phenomenon that is particularly well documented in climate science, where ensembles of competing models routinely produce divergent projections of future temperatures, precipitation, and sea levels (Mehta & Srivastava, 2020).

Practitioners are often acutely aware of model limitations, asking how accurate models are, what they can and cannot produce, and recognising that missing data inputs render the models themselves less effective (Doyle et al., 2023). Yet this awareness does not always translate into explicit communication to end users, who may receive model outputs presented as if they were direct observations of reality rather than outputs of a simplified and imperfect analytical apparatus.

A striking finding from the empirical forecasting literature, documented at length by Makridakis & Bakas (2016), is that more complex models do not reliably produce better predictions. Across a wide range of empirical studies, simple forecasting methods have matched or outperformed sophisticated statistical models in genuine out-of-sample tests. The reason is instructive: complex models are flexible enough to capture noise as well as signal during the estimation phase, but this very flexibility becomes a liability when conditions change — as they inevitably do. In communication terms, this implies that model complexity should not be conflated with model reliability, and that presenting a complex model’s output without acknowledging model uncertainty may convey a false sense of precision.

4.3 Parameter Uncertainty

Closely related to model uncertainty is parameter uncertainty, which concerns the specific numerical values assigned to the variables within a model. Even when the structural form of a model is well specified, its parameters must be estimated from finite and imperfect data. This estimation process introduces uncertainty that propagates through all subsequent outputs.

Parameter uncertainty is perhaps the most commonly acknowledged form of uncertainty in quantitative science — it is the uncertainty captured by confidence intervals, standard errors, and credible intervals in Bayesian analyses. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (n.d.) identifies it as arising from “poorly known or changing model parameters” that govern the equations underpinning projections. In complex risk assessments, parameter uncertainty frequently manifests as “uncertainty about the values of parameters in scientific models,” which must be estimated or calibrated using imperfect historical data (Bles et al., 2019).

However, even this relatively well-understood form of uncertainty is systematically underestimated in practice. Makridakis & Bakas (2016) document that the confidence intervals produced by many standard forecasting methods are systematically too narrow — larger sample sizes do not always reduce forecast uncertainty as much as statistical theory suggests. This underestimation of parameter uncertainty is a direct source of overconfidence in communicated forecasts.

4.4 Structural and Scenario Uncertainty

Structural uncertainty operates at a higher conceptual level than parameter uncertainty. It represents flaws or limitations in the fundamental theoretical architecture of a model — not in the values assigned to its parameters, but in the assumptions about which variables to include, how to define the boundaries of the system, and which processes and relationships to represent. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (n.d.) characterises structural uncertainty as arising from “inadequate models, incomplete or competing conceptual frameworks, lack of agreement on model structure, ambiguous system boundaries or definitions, or significant processes or relationships wrongly specified or not considered.”

Structural uncertainty is particularly insidious because it is the hardest to detect from within the modelling framework itself. A model that is structurally wrong in an important respect may still fit historical data reasonably well, because the fitting process can compensate for structural errors by assigning incorrect values to the parameters that are included. Only when the model is applied in a genuinely novel situation — one that lies outside the envelope of conditions in which it was estimated — do structural errors typically become apparent. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (n.d.) notes explicitly that “experts tend to underestimate structural uncertainty arising from incomplete understanding of or competing conceptual frameworks for relevant systems and processes,” leading to overconfident projections that fail to account for the possibility that the model is wrong in a fundamental way.

Closely related to structural uncertainty is scenario uncertainty, which captures the inherent unpredictability of human behaviour and social evolution. Because human choices — about energy use, political organisation, technological adoption, and economic behaviour — cannot be modelled deterministically, forecasting over sufficiently long horizons must account for divergent possible futures driven by different human decisions. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (n.d.) describes this as “projections of human behaviour not easily amenable to prediction (e.g. evolution of political systems).” In climate modelling, scenario uncertainty encompasses the full range of possible future emission trajectories as a function of hypothetical socioeconomic choices about demographic change and energy policy (Mehta & Srivastava, 2020). Unlike parameter or structural uncertainty, scenario uncertainty cannot in principle be reduced by collecting more data or building better models — it reflects the genuine openness of the future.

4.5 Human and Judgmental Uncertainty

A final and often underappreciated source of uncertainty is introduced by the humans who collect, interpret, and communicate information. This source operates at multiple levels.

At the level of individual scientists, Doyle et al. (2023) document through qualitative interviews with natural hazard researchers that the “human element” is a profound driver of uncertainty. Scientists who prefer specific research designs or methodological approaches may inadvertently limit the scope of available evidence, “creating uncertainty” by the selectivity of what they choose to study. Beyond methodological preferences, uncertainty is generated by interpersonal dynamics: disagreements between researchers, ego-driven conflicts over interpretation, and varying standards of what constitutes rigorous evidence all introduce variability into the knowledge base that cannot be captured by formal uncertainty quantification (Doyle et al., 2023).

At the level of interpretation, it matters deeply “how data are interpreted, when, and by whom” (Doyle et al., 2023). The same dataset can yield different uncertainty assessments in the hands of different analysts, depending on their disciplinary training, their prior beliefs, and their institutional incentives. This interpretive layer of uncertainty is particularly consequential because it is rarely made transparent in published reports or public communications.

At the level of the broader communication ecosystem, Doyle et al. (2023) highlight that the media environment itself functions as an uncertainty generator: “conflicting advice is readily available online,” and the amplification of scientific disagreements through media coverage can produce a level of apparent uncertainty in the public mind that substantially exceeds the genuine epistemic uncertainty in the scientific community.

Finally, human uncertainty includes the systematic cognitive biases that cause forecasters and analysts to misrepresent their own uncertainty. Makridakis & Bakas (2016) document extensive evidence that judgmental forecasts are typically overconfident — that is, the stated confidence intervals are too narrow relative to the actual track record of the forecasts. The same overconfidence has been documented in medical diagnosis, where physicians rated their confidence only marginally lower for difficult cases than for easy ones, even when their diagnostic accuracy dropped from 55% to below 6% (Makridakis et al., 2009). This systematic miscalibration of human uncertainty is one of the strongest arguments for formal uncertainty quantification and explicit uncertainty communication, rather than relying on expert intuition alone.

4.6 Good Practice

  • Use systematic typologies: To prevent critical gaps in uncertainty assessment, practitioners should actively “consider all plausible sources of uncertainty using a systematic typology of uncertainty” rather than relying on ad hoc identification of what seems most salient in a given context (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, n.d.).
  • Audit model assumptions: To address structural and parameter uncertainty, forecasters should “specify assumptions and system definitions clearly, compare models with observations for a range of conditions, and assess maturity of the underlying science” (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, n.d.). Model complexity should not be treated as a proxy for reliability.
  • Interrogate the source before choosing the format: Communicators should explicitly diagnose the source of uncertainty before engaging audiences. The question “why is there uncertainty?” — whether because of natural variation, measurement difficulties, limited process knowledge, or expert disagreement — should shape the choice of communication method and the level of precision that is appropriate (Bles et al., 2019).
  • Acknowledge the human element: Reports and communications should distinguish, where possible, between uncertainty that arises from the system being studied and uncertainty that arises from the choices and limitations of the analysts who studied it. Transparency about the human and institutional sources of uncertainty is itself a form of honest communication.
