3  Conceptualising Uncertainty

The word “uncertainty” is used freely across virtually every academic discipline, yet it carries subtly — and sometimes radically — different meanings depending on the field in which it appears. In philosophy it refers to the limits of knowledge; in physics it can mean the fundamental indeterminacy of quantum mechanics; in statistics it refers to the variability of an estimator; in economics it denotes the impossibility of assigning probabilities to outcomes; in everyday speech it simply means not knowing what will happen. As Makridakis & Bakas (2016) observe, uncertainty “lacks a unique definition and clear meaning that varies when used in different fields such as philosophy, physics, information science, economics, statistics, finance, politics, psychology, engineering and insurance.” This multiplicity of meanings is not merely terminological housekeeping — it has direct practical consequences. When a scientist reports that a finding is “uncertain,” a policymaker, a journalist, and a risk analyst may each understand something quite different by the statement. Conceptual clarity about what uncertainty means, and what it does not mean, is therefore a prerequisite for effective communication.

This section reviews the principal frameworks through which scholars have attempted to bring analytical order to the concept of uncertainty. It begins with the foundational distinction between epistemic and aleatory uncertainty, before broadening to consider ontological, linguistic, and deep uncertainty. It then examines Stirling’s influential matrix of incertitude and the related taxonomy of known and unknown unknowns. The section concludes with a discussion of the key disagreements in the literature and their implications for practice.

3.1 What is Uncertainty?

At its most general, uncertainty refers to a state of incomplete or imperfect knowledge — an inability to fully describe the present or reliably predict the future (Makridakis & Bakas, 2016). This everyday understanding, however, conceals important distinctions. A physician who cannot determine a diagnosis with confidence, a meteorologist issuing a five-day forecast, and an engineer assessing the structural integrity of a bridge under novel loading conditions are all dealing with uncertainty, yet the character of their uncertainty — its source, its reducibility, and the kind of information that would help resolve it — differs substantially in each case.

A critical philosophical distinction that underpins much of the subsequent literature is that uncertainty is fundamentally a property of knowledge, not of the world itself (Scoones, 2019). The future trajectory of a hurricane is not inherently uncertain; it is determined by physical laws. It appears uncertain to us because we lack the observational precision and computational power to fully model those laws. This epistemological framing — uncertainty as a gap in human knowledge rather than an objective feature of reality — has profound implications for how it should be communicated and managed. It suggests that uncertainty can, at least in principle, be reduced through better data, better models, and more careful reasoning, even if it can never be entirely eliminated.

3.2 Epistemic and Aleatory Uncertainty

The most widely used distinction in statistical and scientific practice is between epistemic and aleatory uncertainty, a division that maps roughly onto the contrast between ignorance and irreducible randomness.

Aleatory uncertainty is conceptualised as the inherent, irreducible stochastic variability within a system — the randomness that remains even with perfect knowledge of the underlying processes (Gruber et al., 2023). It represents, in Spiegelhalter (2017)’s formulation, the “inevitable unpredictability of the future due to unforeseeable factors, fully expressed by classical probabilities.” In financial contexts, aleatory uncertainty is closely related to what is termed latent volatility — the inherent fluctuation in asset prices that cannot be predicted regardless of how sophisticated the model (Eggen, 2025). The canonical example is the outcome of a fair coin toss: no amount of additional information about the coin or the person flipping it can, under normal circumstances, allow us to predict the result with certainty.

Epistemic uncertainty, by contrast, refers to a state of incomplete knowledge about the world that could, in theory, be resolved with more data or better models. It encompasses, as Spiegelhalter (2017) describes it, “uncertainty about the structure and parameters of statistical models, expressed, for example, through Bayesian probability distributions,” and is fundamentally reducible through improving models or data (Eggen, 2025). In the language of Bles et al. (2019), epistemic uncertainty concerns “the past and present state of the world that arises because of what we do not know but could know in theory.” A physician who is unsure of a diagnosis because a test result is not yet available faces epistemic uncertainty; the same physician uncertain about a patient’s idiosyncratic biological response to a treatment confronts something closer to aleatory uncertainty.

This distinction is practically significant because it implies different responses. Epistemic uncertainty motivates investment in better measurement, broader data collection, and more refined models. Aleatory uncertainty, by contrast, calls for honest acknowledgement of irreducible limits and for communication strategies that convey the range of possible outcomes rather than implying a determinism that does not exist.

However, the boundary between the two is less clean than it first appears. Gruber et al. (2023) argue forcefully that a simple decomposition into aleatory and epistemic uncertainty “does not do justice to the overarching problem of multiple sources of uncertainty.” Their central challenge is that the classification of uncertainty as aleatory or epistemic is entirely context-dependent, not a universal property of the phenomenon being studied. A dice roll, for instance, is conventionally treated as purely aleatory; yet if one possessed a model that perfectly measured the initial position, velocity, and rotation of the die, the outcome would become entirely predictable — effectively epistemic. As Gruber et al. (2023) conclude, “uncertainties can therefore only be distinguished on a model and data basis and not universally.” This insight is important for communication: claiming that a source of uncertainty is irreducibly aleatory may be more a statement about the limits of current models than about the nature of reality.

3.3 Ontological, Linguistic, and Deep Uncertainty

Beyond the epistemic-aleatory divide, scholars have identified further conceptual layers of uncertainty that resist standard probabilistic treatment.

Ontological uncertainty acknowledges the ultimate limits of mathematical modelling as a representation of a complex reality. Spiegelhalter (2017) defines it as “uncertainty about the entire modelling process as a description of reality” — the possibility that no model, however sophisticated, fully captures the dynamics of the system being studied. This form of uncertainty is particularly relevant in complex socio-technical or socio-ecological systems, where the interactions between human behaviour, institutional structures, and natural processes cannot be fully specified in advance (Scoones, 2019).

Linguistic uncertainty arises from the limits of human language in describing and communicating risk and uncertainty. It manifests as vagueness, where a term has imprecise boundaries; ambiguity, where a term has multiple possible meanings; and under-specificity, where a statement does not provide sufficient detail to constrain interpretation (Unknown Author, n.d.). Linguistic uncertainty is particularly prominent in qualitative risk assessments, where terms such as “low risk” or “possible” are routinely used without explicit reference to exposure conditions or probability scales, leaving audiences to fill in the gaps with their own prior beliefs.

Deep uncertainty defines a more radical condition in which the fundamental parameters of a problem cannot be agreed upon. Doyle et al. (2023) characterise it as a situation that “exists when experts or stakeholders do not know or cannot agree on conceptual models, uncertainty probability distributions, and how to weigh and value desirable alternative outcomes.” Deep uncertainty is not merely a quantitative problem — it is a problem of contested knowledge and competing value systems. Standard probabilistic tools are inadequate under conditions of deep uncertainty, and communication strategies must shift accordingly, from conveying precise probabilities to facilitating deliberation about alternative possible futures.

3.4 Stirling’s Matrix: The Four Dimensions of Incertitude

One of the most influential conceptual frameworks for understanding the multidimensional character of uncertainty is the incertitude matrix proposed by Andy Stirling, which provides a more rigorous taxonomy than the catch-all term “uncertainty” alone can supply (Scoones, 2019; Stirling, n.d.b). The matrix is constructed on two axes: whether knowledge about outcomes (what could happen) is problematic or unproblematic, and whether knowledge about likelihoods (how probable each outcome is) is problematic or unproblematic. Crossing these two dimensions yields four distinct states of incertitude, each of which implies a different analytical and communicative response.

Risk occupies the quadrant where both outcomes and their probabilities can be reliably estimated (Scoones, 2019). This is the domain of classical probabilistic risk assessment — rolling dice, actuarial insurance tables, and well-studied engineering failure modes. In this condition, standard statistical tools are appropriate, and uncertainty can be quantified and communicated as a probability distribution or confidence interval.

Uncertainty, in the strict Knightian sense, describes conditions where possible outcomes are known but there is no reliable basis for assigning probabilities to them (Stirling, n.d.b). One might know that a new drug could be effective or ineffective, or that a policy intervention could succeed or fail, without having the empirical basis to attach meaningful probabilities to these possibilities. In this condition, probability estimates are formally illegitimate, and presenting them as though they were meaningful constitutes a “pretence of knowledge” (Stirling, n.d.a).

Ambiguity arises when probabilities might in principle be calculable, but there is fundamental disagreement about how to define or frame the possible outcomes (Scoones, 2019). This condition is common in contested policy domains — such as the risk of genetically modified organisms, or the long-term consequences of emerging technologies — where different scientific communities or stakeholder groups organise the problem space in incompatible ways.

Ignorance represents the most challenging condition: one in which neither the outcomes nor their probabilities can be fully characterised, and where parameters are “not only contestable but also — at least in part — unknown” (Stirling, n.d.a). This is the domain of “unknown unknowns” — genuinely unanticipated effects and emergent phenomena that fall entirely outside the existing analytical frame. Black Swan events, as described by Makridakis & Bakas (2016), are paradigmatic examples.

A crucial epistemological point about this matrix, emphasised by both Stirling (n.d.b) and Scoones (2019), is that these four conditions are states of knowledge, not states of nature. They describe the relationship between an observer and a system, not objective properties of the system itself. The same phenomenon may be treated as a risk by a well-resourced modelling team with extensive historical data and as ignorance by a community with no access to such information. This relativity of uncertainty states has profound implications for governance and communication: it means that characterising a situation as “risky” rather than “uncertain” or “ambiguous” is always, to some degree, a social and political act, not merely a technical one.

3.5 Known and Unknown Unknowns

A complementary taxonomy that has gained wide currency in policy and forecasting contexts is the classification of uncertainty into four quadrants based on the interaction of what is known and unknown about both outcomes and mechanisms (Makridakis & Bakas, 2016). This framework, popularised through Donald Rumsfeld’s 2002 press conference but with deeper roots in decision theory, distinguishes between:

  • Known/Knowns: Situations where both outcomes and their likelihoods are understood. Standard statistical forecasting applies, uncertainty is measurable, and risk can be estimated within acceptable bounds.
  • Unknown/Knowns: Situations where relevant knowledge exists but is ignored, suppressed, or denied — often due to institutional pressures, cognitive biases, or wilful blindness. This is arguably the most dangerous category, as it involves knowable information being rendered operationally invisible.
  • Known/Unknowns: Situations where the forecaster is aware of their ignorance — they know they do not know. Uncertainty is large, often fat-tailed, and planning must proceed without reliable probability estimates.
  • Unknown/Unknowns: Genuinely unanticipated events that fall entirely outside the existing frame of analysis. These are Black Swan events that cannot be predicted by definition, but whose consequences can sometimes be moderated through robustness and adaptability strategies.

Makridakis et al. (2009) introduce a memorable distinction between two limiting cases of this taxonomy. Subway uncertainty represents the “known unknowns” that operate in controlled, well-understood environments — like the random delay of a subway train — where standard statistical models apply and uncertainty can be quantified. Coconut uncertainty, by contrast, encompasses the “unknown unknowns” characteristic of rare, catastrophic events — like a fatal accident caused by a falling coconut — where the event is “impossible to quantify or model… even though we can clearly expect these to occur.” This distinction highlights that not all uncertainty can be managed by the same tools, and that the aspiration to reduce all uncertainty to measurable risk is, in many domains, fundamentally misconceived.

3.6 Disagreements and Tensions in the Literature

Two significant tensions run through the conceptual literature on uncertainty and are worth making explicit.

The first concerns the epistemological status of the aleatory-epistemic distinction. While this division is widely taught in statistics and machine learning curricula, Gruber et al. (2023) argue that it is philosophically and mathematically untenable as a universal classification. Because whether a source of variation is treated as aleatory or epistemic depends on the model and data available rather than on any intrinsic property of the phenomenon, the distinction is always provisional. This challenges a common assumption in uncertainty communication — that some uncertainty is “irreducible” and must simply be reported rather than investigated. In reality, what counts as irreducible is always relative to the current state of knowledge.

The second concerns the institutional treatment of uncertainty. Stirling (n.d.a) documents a troubling pattern in which regulatory and environmental institutions, facing political pressure to produce clear and actionable numbers, routinely describe what is genuinely deep uncertainty as if it were calculable risk. The same word — “uncertainty” — is used to describe both a situation where probabilities are unknown and a situation where a specific probability has been confidently asserted. This institutional conflation, characterised as a “pretence of knowledge,” has the effect of foreclosing deliberation about alternative possibilities and concentrating decision-making authority in technical hands. For communicators, the practical implication is clear: the choice of how to frame uncertainty — as risk, as uncertainty, as ambiguity, or as ignorance — is never a purely technical decision, and its consequences deserve careful scrutiny.

3.7 Good Practice

  • Resist reductionism: Practitioners should resist the temptation to close down complex uncertainty, ambiguity, or ignorance into simple probabilistic risk assessments. Applying risk-based mathematical approaches to deeply uncertain situations is, as Stirling (n.d.a) argues, “irrational, unscientific and potentially misleading.”
  • Address linguistic uncertainty: In qualitative assessments, communicators must actively minimise linguistic uncertainty by explicitly specifying the context in which probability terms are used, ensuring that exposure variables and reference classes are clearly defined (Unknown Author, n.d.).
  • Track the cascade of uncertainty: When developing models or assessments, analysts should explicitly categorise uncertainty across three dimensions — its location in the analytical pipeline, its nature (aleatory or epistemic), and its level (from determinism to deep ignorance) — to maintain a clear audit trail of assumptions (Pascoe et al., n.d.).
  • State clearly what type of uncertainty is being communicated: Before choosing a communication format, identify whether the uncertainty at hand is aleatory, epistemic, structural, or deep, as each type implies different appropriate formats and levels of precision, as detailed in Chapter 8.
